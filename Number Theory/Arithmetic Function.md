An **arithmetic (number-theoretic) function** is any function $f:\mathbb Z_{\ge1}\to\mathbb C$. Hardy & Wright include in their definition the requirement that an arithmetical function "expresses some arithmetical property of $n$".
- **Additivity/Multiplicativity**: an arithmetic function $f$ is **additive/multiplicative** if $$f(m+n)=f(m)+f(n)\quad\text{or}\quad f(mn)=f(m)f(n),\quad\forall (m,n)=1$$It's called **completely additive/multiplicative** if the above holds for any $m,n\in\mathbb Z_{\ge1}$.
## $p$-adic valuation
For $n\in\mathbb N,p\in\mathbb P$ define the **[[p-adic Field]] valuation function** $$\nu_p(n)=\begin{cases}\max\set{t\in\mathbb N:p^k\mid n},&n\neq0\\\infty,&n=0\end{cases}$$Given $n=p_1^{\alpha_1}\cdots p_k^{\alpha_k}$ define the **prime omega functions** $$\omega(n)=k,\quad\Omega(n)=\sum_{i=1}^k\alpha_k$$These notations are extremely commonly used in number theory.
- **Legendre's formula**: we have $$\nu_p(n!)=\sum_{n=1}^\infty$$
## Euler's totient function
The Euler's totient function $\varphi(n)$ is defined as $$\varphi(n)=\left|\mathbb Z_n^\times\right|=\#\set{1\le m<n:(m,n)=1}$$that is, the [[Cardinality]] of the reduced residue system ([[Modular Arithmetic]]).
- **Euler's product formula**: we have the expression $$\varphi(n)=n\prod_{p\mid n,p\in\mathbb P}\left(1-\frac{1}{p}\right)$$**Proof**: by property of the residual system ([[Modular Arithmetic]]) when $\gcd(m,n)=1$ we have $$\mathbb Z_m\times\mathbb Z_n\cong\mathbb Z_{mn}$$hence $\varphi(mn)=\varphi(m)\varphi(n)$, which means that $\varphi$ is multiplicative. By the fundamental theorem of arithmetic ([[Division]]) it suffices to calculate $\varphi(p^k),p\in\mathbb P$, and this is quite straightforward: $$\mathbb Z_{p^k}^\times=[p^k]\left\backslash\Set{kp:1\le k\le p^{k-1}}\right.\quad\Longrightarrow\quad\varphi(p^k)=p^k-p^{k-1}=p^k\left(1-\frac{1}{p}\right)$$hence for any $n\in\mathbb N$ we have $$\varphi(n)=\prod_{p\mid n,p\in\mathbb P}\varphi\left(p^{\nu_p(n)}\right)=\prod_{p\mid n,p\in\mathbb P}p^{\nu_p(n)}\left(1-\frac{1}{p}\right)=n\prod_{p\mid n,p\in\mathbb P}\left(1-\frac{1}{p}\right)$$this is exactly the desired expression.
	- **Proof via inclusion-exclusion principle**: the result can also be proved via the inclusion-exclusion principle ([[Set Theory]]), by ruling out from $[n]$ the sets of integers divisible by $p\mid n,p\in\mathbb P$.
- **Euler's theorem**: for any $(a,n)=1$ we have $$a^{\varphi(n)}\equiv1\pmod{n}$$**Proof**: since $\mathbb Z_n^\times$ ([[Modular Arithmetic]]) forms a group under multiplication, we have $$a\mathbb Z_n^\times=\mathbb Z_n^\times\quad\Longrightarrow\quad\prod_{x\in\mathbb Z_n^\times}x\equiv\prod_{x\in\mathbb Z_n^\times}ax\equiv a^{\varphi(n)}\prod_{x\in\mathbb Z_n^\times}x\pmod n$$by dividing the product on both sides we obtain the desired result.
	- **Generalization**: Euler's theorem is actually a special case of Lagrange's theorem ([[Group]]) applied on the $\braket{a}\le\mathbb Z_n^\times$.
- **Divisor sum**: for any $n\in\mathbb N$ we have the equality established by Gauss $$\sum_{d\mid n}\varphi(d)=n$$**Proof**: notice that we can partition $[1,n]$ based on their GCD ([[Division]]) with $n$: $$n=\sum_{k=1}^n1=\sum_{d\mid n}\sum_{1\le k\le n,\gcd(k,n)=d}1=\sum_{d\mid n}\sum_{1\le k\le n,\gcd(k,n/d)=1}1=\sum_{d\mid n}\varphi\left(\frac{n}{d}\right)=\sum_{d\mid n}\varphi(d)$$Note that we can realize this partition by grouping $\frac{k}{n},1\le k\le n$ according to the enumerator in their reduced forms. 
	- **Corollary**: by applying the Möbius inversion formula (below) we have $$\varphi(n)=\sum_{d\mid n}\mu(d)\cdot\frac{n}{d}=n\sum_{d\mid n}\frac{\mu(d)}{d}$$Note that this can also be derived directly from the product formula. 
## Möbius function
The Möbius function is defined by $$\mu(n)=\begin{cases}1,&\text{if }n=1\\(-1)^{\omega(n)},&\text{if }\nu_p(n)=1,\forall p\mid n,p\in\mathbb P\\0,&\text{if }\nu_p(n)>1\text{ for some }p\mid n,p\in\mathbb P\end{cases}$$It can also be represented as $\mu(n)=\delta_{\omega(n)}^{\Omega(n)}\lambda(n)$ where $\lambda(n)$ is the Liouville function. It's obvious from definition that $\mu$ is multiplicative. 
- **Möbius inversion formula**: notice that we have the summation $$\sum_{d\mid n}\mu(d)=\delta_n^1,\quad\forall n\in\mathbb Z_{\ge1}$$hence for any pair of arithmetic functions $f,g$ we have the **Möbius inversion formula** $$g(n)=\sum_{d\mid n}f(d),\quad\forall n\in\mathbb Z_{\ge1}\quad\iff\quad f(n)=\sum_{d\mid n}\mu(d)g\left(\frac{n}{d}\right),\quad\forall n\in\mathbb Z_{\ge1}$$**Proof**: the summation formula can be easily verified via the fundamental theorem of arithmetics ([[Division]]), and utilizing it we have $$\begin{align}f(n)&=\sum_{d\mid n}f(d)\delta_{n/d}^1=\sum_{d\mid n}f(d)\sum_{d'\mid n/d}\mu(d')\\&=\sum_{d'd\mid n}f(d)\mu(d')=\sum_{d'\mid n}\mu(d')\sum_{d\mid n/d'}f(d)\\&=\sum_{d'\mid n}\mu(d')g\left(\frac{n}{d'}\right)\end{align}$$Here we used a change of order of summation in the deduction. This idea can also be seen in the inversion formula for [[Fourier Series]] and [[FT on L2]]. 
## Floor & ceiling function
For $x\in\mathbb R$ the **floor** and **ceiling functions** are defined by $$\floor{x}=\max\set{n\in\mathbb Z:n\ge x},\quad\ceil{x}=\min\set{n\in\mathbb Z:n\ge x}$$Based on this we also define the **sawtooth function** $$\set{x}=x-\floor x,\quad\forall x\in\mathbb R$$many properties of these functions follows easily from the definitions.
- **$p$-adic valuation of factorial**: by a simple change of order of summation we have $$\nu_p(n!)=\sum_{j=1}^\infty\floor{\frac{n}{p^j}},\quad\forall p\in\mathbb P$$this is useful when dealing with division problems involving factorials.
## Dirichlet ring
Given two arithmetic functions, their **Dirichlet [[Convolution]]** is defined as $$(f*g)(n)=\sum_{d\mid n}f(d)g\left(\frac{n}{d}\right)=\sum_{ab=n}f(a)g(b)$$This definition is motivated by the (Cauchy) product ([[Sequence]]) of Dirichlet series, in which it arises naturally by $$\left(\sum_{n\ge1}\frac{f(n)}{n^s}\right)\left(\sum_{n\ge1}\frac{g(n)}{n^s}\right)=\sum_{n\ge1}\frac{(f*g)(n)}{n^s}$$Now consider the set of arithmetic functions with pointwise addition and multiplication defined by Dirichlet convolution. We can verify easily that it's a commutative [[Ring]], which is called the **Dirichlet ring**, with $\varepsilon(n)=\delta_n^1$ being the multiplicative identity. 
- **Units**: the multiplicatively invertible elements are those $f$ with $f(1)\neq0$. Their multiplicative inverse is also called the **Dirichlet inverse**.b
