Hardy space is motivated by the need to study the boundary behavior of bounded [[Holomorphic Function]]. This can also be seen as a questions asking what kind of function on $\mathbb R$ has [[Analytic Continuation]].
## Hardy space on the unit disk
Denote $\mathbb D=\set{|z|<1}$ the unit disk, then $H^p(\mathbb D)$ consists of all holomorphic functions satisfying $$\|F\|_{H^P(\mathbb D)}=\sup_{0\le r<1}\left(\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}|F(re^{i\theta})|^pd\theta\right)^{1/p}<\infty$$
- **Fatou's theorem**: a bounded holomorphic function $F(re^{i\theta})$ on the unit disk $D=\set{|z|<1}$ has radial limit $\lim_{r\nearrow1}F(re^{i\theta})$ for $\ae\theta$.
  **Proof**: consider the Laurent series $F(re^{i\theta})=\sum a_nr^ne^{in\theta},r<1$, we know that it converges absolutely and uniformly ([[Holomorphic Function]]). Notice that $a_nr^n$ is also the Fourier coefficient of $F(re^{i\theta})$, pick $|F(z)|\le M,\forall z\in D$, then by Parseval's identity $$\sum_{n\in\mathbb N}|a_n|^2r^{2n}=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^\pi |F(re^{i\theta})|^2d\theta,\quad\forall0\le r<1$$Taking $r\to1$ yields $\sum|a_n|^2\le M^2$. Now let $F(e^{i\theta})$ be an $L^2$ function with Fourier coefficients $a_n$, then the result follows from the following lemma.
	- **Abel summability of Fourier series**: let $f\in L^2([-\pi,\pi])$, then $\sum_{-\infty}^\infty a_nr^{|n|}e^{inx}\to f(x)$ for $\ae x$ as $r\nearrow1$, where $a_n=\hat f(n)$.
	  **Proof**: notice the Poisson kernel formula $\sum r^{|n|}e^{iny}=P_r(y)=\frac{1-r^2}{1-2r\cos y+r^2}$, extend $f$ to $\mathbb R$ periodically, then by DCT we have $$\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^\pi f(x-y)P_r(y)dy=\sum_{-\infty}^\infty r^{|n|}\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}f(x-y)e^{iny}dy=\sum_{-\infty}^\infty a_nr^{|n|}e^{inx}$$Now since $\set{P_r(y)}_r$ is an approximation to the identity ([[Convolution]]) the limit holds for Lebesgue points, i.e., $\ae x$.
## Hardy space on the upper half plane
Denote $\mathbb R_+^2=\set{z=x+iy:x\in\mathbb R,y>0}$, the hardy space $H^2(\mathbb R_+^2)$ consists of all holomorphic functions with finite norm $$\|F\|_{H^2(\mathbb R_+^2)}=\sup_{y>0}\left(\int_\mathbb R|F(x+iy)|^2dx\right)^{1/2}<\infty$$Given any $\hat F_0\in L^2(0,\infty)$ consider $$F(x+iy)=\int_0^\infty\hat F_0(\xi)e^{2\pi i\xi z}d\xi,\quad z=x+iy,y>0$$then it's obvious that the above integral converges absolutely and uniformly on $y\ge\delta>0$ by Cauchy-Schwarz inequality ([[Lp Space]]), hence $F$ is holomorphic on $\mathbb R_+^2$ ([[Complex Function]]). Moreover by MCT $$\|F\|_{H^2(\mathbb R_+^2)}^2=\sup_{y>0}\int_\mathbb R|F(x+iy)|^2dx=\sup_{y>0}\int_0^\infty|\hat F_0(\xi)|^2e^{-4\pi\xi y}d\xi=\|\hat F_0\|_2^2$$where the second equality follows from the Plancherel's theorem ([[FT on L2]]).
- **Integral expression of $H^2(\mathbb R_+^2)$**: any $F\in H^2(\mathbb R_+^2)$ can be expressed as $$F(x+iy)=\int_0^\infty\hat F_0(\xi)e^{2\pi i\xi z}d\xi,\quad z=x+iy,y>0$$with $\hat F_0\in L^2(0,\infty)$, and $\|F\|_{H^2}=\|\hat F_0\|_2$.
  **Proof**: integrate Gauss's mean value property ([[Cauchy Integral]]) over $r$ we get $$F(\zeta)=\frac{1}{\pi\delta^2}\int_{|z|<\delta}F(\zeta+z)dxdy,\quad z=x+iy,\Im\zeta>\delta$$Write $\zeta=\xi+i\eta,\eta>\delta$, then the disk $B_\delta(\zeta)$ is contained in $\set{z+\zeta:z=x+iy,-\delta<y<\delta}$ which also lies in $\mathbb R_+^2$, hence $$\begin{align}|F(\zeta)|^2&\le\left(\frac{1}{\pi\delta^2}\int_{|z|<\delta}|F(\zeta+z)|\right)^2&\text{Triangle inequality}\\&\le\frac{1}{\pi\delta^2}\int_{|z|<\delta}|F(\zeta+z)|^2dxdy&\text{Cauchy-Schwarz inequality}\\&\le\frac{1}{\pi\delta^2}\int_{|y|<\delta}\int_\mathbb R|F(\zeta+x+iy)|^2dxdy&\text{Monotonicity of integral}\\&\le\frac{2}{\pi\delta}\sup_{-\delta<y<\delta}\int_\mathbb R|F(x+i(\eta+y))|^2dx\\&\le\frac{2}{\pi\delta}\sup_{y>0}\int_\mathbb R|F(x+iy)|^2dx&\text{since }\eta>\delta\\&=\frac{2}{\pi\delta}\|F\|_{H^2}^2\end{align}$$Hence $|F(\zeta)|$ is bounded on $\Im\zeta>0$. Now consider $$F^\epsilon(z)=F(z)\frac{1}{(1-i\epsilon z)^2}\to F(z),\quad\forall\Im z>0,\epsilon\searrow0$$We have $|F^\epsilon(z)|<|F(z)|,\forall \Im z>0$, hence by DCT $F^\epsilon(x+iy)\xrightarrow{L^2}F(x+iy)$ given any $y>0$, and moreover by boundedness of $|F(z)|$ we have $|F^\epsilon(z)|=O(\frac{1}{1+x^2}),\forall\Im z\ge\delta>0$. Below we'll first show that the integral expression holds for $F^\epsilon$, and then generalize to $F$. Given $y$ denote $\hat F_y$ the FT of $F(x+iy)$. For $G(z)=F^\epsilon(z)e^{-2\pi iz\xi}$ by uniform decay condition we can consider a rectangle [[Contour Integral]] to show that $$\int_{\mathbb R+iy_1}G(z)dz=\int_{\mathbb R+iy_2}G(z)dz\quad\Longrightarrow\quad\hat F_{y_1}^\epsilon(\xi)e^{2\pi y_1\xi}=\hat F_{y_2}^\epsilon(\xi)e^{2\pi y_2\xi}$$since $F^\epsilon(x+iy)\xrightarrow{L^2}F(x+iy),\epsilon\to0$ we obtain $\hat F_{y_1}(\xi)e^{2\pi y_1\xi}=\hat F_{y_2}(\xi)e^{2\pi y_2\xi},\ae\xi$. This means $\hat F_y(\xi)e^{2\pi y\xi}$ is independent of $y$, hence there exists some $\hat F_0(\xi)$ s.t. $$\hat F_y(\xi)=\hat F_0(\xi)e^{-2\pi\xi y},\quad\forall y>0$$By Plancherel's identity ([[FT on L2]]) we have $$\sup_{y>0}\int_\mathbb R|\hat F_0(\xi)|^2e^{-4\pi\xi y}d\xi=\sup_{y>0}\int_\mathbb R|F(x+iy)|^2dx=\|F\|_{H^2}^2<\infty$$This implies $\hat F_0(\xi)=0,\ae\xi\in(-\infty,0)$, otherwise we can let $y\to\infty$ to yields infinity. Finally since LHS equals $\|\hat F_0\|_2^2$ we have $\hat F_0\in L^2(0,\infty)$, we can apply inversion formula ([[FT on L2]]) to conclude the result. 
	- **Corollary**: $H^2(\mathbb R_+^2)\cong L^2(0,\infty)$ with the above integral expression bing a isometry. 
	- **Intuition**: in Hardy space, the function is completely determined by its boundary values, and these boundary values are half-sided in the frequency domain. We introduce $F^\epsilon$ to ensure that it decays fast enough at infinite **uniformly** in $y$, so that Jordan's lemma can be applied on any pair $y_1,y_2>0$.
- **Boundary-value function**: for $F\in H^2(\mathbb R_+^2)$ we have $\lim_{y\to0}F(x+iy)=F_0(x)$ as both an $L^2$ and $\ae$ limit.
  **Proof**: $L^2$ convergence follows directly from Plancherel's identity and the above result. By convolution theorem ([[FT on L2]]) we have the $$\int_\mathbb R\hat f(\xi)e^{-2\pi|\xi|y}e^{2\pi i\xi x}d\xi=\int_\mathbb R f(x-t)\mathcal P_y(t)dt,\quad\forall f\in L^2(\mathbb R)$$where $\mathcal P_y(x)$ is the Poisson kernel ([[Convolution#Poisson kernel]]). Since $F=\int\hat F_0e^{2\pi i\xi z}d\xi$ with $\hat F_0(\xi)=0,\forall\xi<0$, by the above identity applied on $F$ we have $$F(x+iy)=\int_\mathbb R F_0(x-t)\mathcal P_y(t)dt$$Notice that $\set{\mathcal P_y(t)}_{y>0}$ is a set of approximation to the identity ([[Convolution]]) we conclude that $F(x+iy)\to F_0(x),\ae x$ (we need a simple localization argument and estimation since the approximation applies to $L^1$ functions).
	- **Relation with [[Cauchy Integral]]**: let $S\subset L^2$ consists of all $F_0$ arising in the above result, then $\hat F_0$ is supported on $(0,\infty)$, hence $S$ is closed.  Denote $P=P_S$ the orthogonal projection onto this subspace ([[Hilbert Space]]), then by definition $\widehat{(Pf)}(\xi)=\chi_{(0,\infty)}(\xi)\hat f(\xi)$. Moreover, denote $F\in H^2(\mathbb R_+^2)$ the function whose boundary function is $P(f)$, then $$F(z)=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_\mathbb R\frac{f(\zeta)}{\zeta-z}d\zeta,\quad \Im z>0$$The proof is similar to that of the Poisson integral: notice that $$\int_0^\infty e^{2\pi i\xi z}d\xi=-\frac{1}{2\pi iz},\quad\forall\Im z>0$$hence convolution theorem yields $$\int_0^\infty\hat f(\xi)e^{-2\pi\xi y} e^{2\pi i\xi x}d\xi=-\int_\mathbb Rf(\zeta)\frac{1}{2\pi(x+iy-\zeta)}d\zeta=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_\mathbb R\frac{f(\zeta)}{\zeta-z}d\zeta$$Taking $f=F_0$ yields exactly the Cauchy integral of $F_0$. Conversely a Cauchy integral can also be transformed into $\int_0^\infty \hat f(z)e^{2\pi i\xi z}\xi$.
