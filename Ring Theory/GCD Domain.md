An [[Integral Domain]] $R$ is a **GCD domain** if for every pair of elements $a, b \in R$, there exists a **greatest common divisor**: $$\gcd(a, b)\in R\qstq\ c\mid a,c\mid b\implies \gcd(a,b)\mid c$$Note that $\gcd(a, b)$ is unique up to multiplication by a unit in $R^\times$. 
- **Position in the hierarchy of integral domain**: the GCD domain occupies a critical middle ground in the hierarchy of integral domains: $$\text{PID} \subsetneq \text{UFD} \subsetneq \text{GCD Domain} \subsetneq \text{Schreier Domain} \subsetneq \text{Integral Domain}$$The concept abstracts the "arithmetic" properties of $\mathbb{Z}$ and $F[X]$ (like Euclid's Lemma) without necessitating that every element decomposes into a _finite_ product of atoms.
	- **"UFD minus ACCP" perspective**: a GCD domain is essentially a UFD that may lack the **ascending chain condition on principal ideals (ACCP)**: $$R \text{ is a UFD} \qiffq R \text{ is a GCD domain and satisfies ACCP.}$$
- **Primary application**: GCD domains are the most general setting in which Gauss's lemma for polynomials holds: if $R$ is a GCD domain, then $R[X]$ is also a GCD domain, and the product of two primitive polynomials is primitive.
## Properties
The utility of a GCD domain lies in its ability to emulate UFD behavior regarding primes and intersections.
- **Basic properties of GCD**: almost all properties from [[Division]] can be directly inherited here since they only rely on the basic ring structure of $\mathbb Z$.
	- **Commutativity**: $\gcd(a,b)\sim\gcd(b,a)$.
	- **Idempotency**: $\gcd(a,a)\sim a$.
	- **Associativity**: by definition we can verify that $$\gcd(a,\gcd(b,c))\sim\gcd(\gcd(a,b),c)\sim\gcd(a,b,c)$$This is the key property for many applications of GCD. 
	- **GCD of multiple elements**: associativity allows us to define $$\gcd(a_1,\cdots,a_n)\in R\qstq c\mid a_i,\forall i\imply\gcd(a_1,\cdots,a_n)\mid c$$which can be computed recursively in any order. 
	- **GCD distributive law**: by law of cancelation ([[Integral Domain]]), $$\gcd(ma_1,\cdots,ma_n)\sim m\gcd(a_1,\cdots,a_n),\quad\forall m\neq0$$This is also referred to as the factorization property.
	- **Preservation of coprime-ness under multiplication**: we have the implication $$(a,b_i)\sim1,\forall i\imply(a,b_1\cdots b_n)\sim1$$This follows from the distributive law with some straightforward calculation. 
- **Principal ideals intersection**: in an integral domain $R$, by definition we have $$\exists\gcd(a,b)\qiffq(a)\cap(b)=(m)\text{ for some }m\in R$$In this case we have $$m = \text{lcm}(a, b),\quad \gcd(a, b) \cdot \text{lcm}(a, b) \sim ab$$where $\sim$ denotes associates.
- **Equivalence of irreducible & prime elements**: for $R$ a GCD domain, we have $$p\in\irr R\qiffq p\text{ is prime}$$**Proof**: $(\Leftarrow)$ holds for any [[Integral Domain]]; for $(\Rightarrow)$, take $p\in\irr R,p\mid ab$, then we have $$\gcd(p,a)\mid p\imply \gcd(p,a)\sim1\text{ or }\gcd(p,a)\sim p$$Similarly, $\gcd(p,b)\sim 1$ or $\gcd(p,b)\sim p$. Notice that $$\gcd(p,a)\sim\gcd(p,b)\sim1\imply \gcd(p,ab)\sim1$$which contradicts the division assumption, therefore we can assume WLOG that $$\gcd(p,a)\sim p\imply p\mid a$$This shows that $p$ is indeed prime. 
- **GCD of one-exception products of pairwise coprime elements**: assume that $a_i,1\le i\le n$ is a collection of elements that are pairwise coprime. We have $$\gcd(b_1,\cdots,b_n)\sim1\where b_i=\prod_{j\neq i}a_i$$**Proof**: we use induction on $n$. The case of $n=2$ is trivial; assuming validity for $n-1$, then by distributive law and preservation of coprime-ness, $$\gcd(b_1,\cdots,b_n)=\gcd(\gcd(b_1,\cdots,b_{n-1}),b_n)=\gcd(a_n,a_1\cdots a_{n-1})=1$$Therefore the induction is completed. 
- **Primal property**: every element $a$ in a GCD domain is **primal**. An element $a$ is primal if whenever $a \mid bc$, there exist $a_1, a_2$ such that $a = a_1 a_2$ with $a_1 \mid b$ and $a_2 \mid c$.
    - **Tool:** This allows for "lifting" divisibility properties through products, a technique essential in commutative algebra and the study of R-modules.
## Examples
- **Ring of entire functions**: consider the ring of entire functions ([[Holomorphic Function]]) $\mathcal O(\mathbb C)$
	- **Insight (Algebraic):** For any two functions $f, g$, a $\gcd$ exists and its zeros (with multiplicity) are the intersection of the zeros of $f$ and $g$. This is guaranteed by the **Weierstrass Factorization Theorem**.
	- **Insight (Non-UFD):** It fails to be a UFD because an entire function can have infinitely many zeros (e.g., $\sin(z)$), violating the finite factorization requirement of UFDs. It is also not Noetherian, as one can construct an infinite chain of ideals $I_n = \langle \sin(z/2^n) \rangle$.
- **Extended integers $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-5}]$**: consider the domain $R = \mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-5}]$. This is a classic example of an integral domain that is not a GCD domain. Observe the two factorizations of $6$: $$6 = 2 \cdot 3 = (1 + \sqrt{-5})(1 - \sqrt{-5})$$The elements $2, 3, 1 \pm \sqrt{-5}$ are all irreducible in $R$.
	- **Angle A (Prime vs Irreducible):** If $R$ were a GCD domain, the irreducible $2$ would have to be prime. However, $2 \mid (1 + \sqrt{-5})(1 - \sqrt{-5})$ but $2 \nmid (1 \pm \sqrt{-5})$. Since an irreducible is not prime, $R$ cannot be a GCD domain.
	- **Angle B (Explicit GCD failure):** Attempting to find $\gcd(6, 2+2\sqrt{-5})$ reveals that no "greatest" common divisor exists. While $2$ is a common divisor, there is no common divisor that all others divide.
