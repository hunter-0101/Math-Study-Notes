Given [[Ring]] $R$, a **left $R$-module** is an set $M$ equipped with an addition $+:M\times M\to M$ and a scaler multiplication $\cdot:R\times M\to M$, denoted $rm$, s.t. for any $r,s\in R,m,n\in M$:
1. **Group structure**: $(M,+)$ is an abelian [[Group]].
2. **Distributivity**: $(r+s)m=rm+sm,r(m+n)=rm+rn$.
3. **Compatibility**: $(rs)m=r(sm)$.
4. **Module identity**: $1x=x$.
A module can be considered as a pre-[[Vector Space]], can be turned into one by improve the structure of scalers from a ring to a field. If a vector space itself possesses a ring structure, then it's further turned into an algebra ([[Field]]). Their relations can be illustrated as below:

- **Submodule**: for an $R$-module $M$, an $R$-submodule is a subgroup $N\le M$ closed under the actions of $R$.
	- Finite union/intersection/sum of submodules is still submodule.
	- **Submodule generated by a subset**: for $A\subset M$ define **submodule generated by $A$** as $$RA=\set{r_1a_1+\cdots+r_ma_m|r_i\in R,a_i\in A,m\in \mathbb Z^+}$$here $A$ is called the generator of $N=RA$. If $|A|=1$ then $RA$ is called **cyclic**.
- **Module homomorphism**: a map $\phi:M\to N$ is an $R$-module homomorphism if it preserves addition within $M$ and action of $R$. i.e., $\phi(rx+y)=r\phi(x)+\phi(y),\forall r\in R,x,y\in M$. 
	- **Homomorphism ring**: $\hom_R(M,N)$ the set of all homomorphisms between modules forms a ring under addition and composition. When $R$ is commutative $\hom_R(M,N)$ is an $R$-algebra.
- **Bimodule**: given rings $S,R$, an **$(S,R)$-bimodule** is an abelian group $M$ that is both a left $S$-module and a right $R$-module with $s(mr)=(sm)r$. 
	- **Standard module**: for $R$ commutative, a standard $R$-module is an $(R,R)$-bimodule.
- **Quotient module**: for $R$-module $M$ and submodule $N$, the (additive, abelian) quotient group $M/N$ can be made into an $R$-module by defining an action of elements of $R$ by $$r(x+N)=(rx)+N,\quad\forall r\in R,x+N\in M/N$$and the natural projection $\pi:M\to M/N$ by $\pi(x)=x+N$ is an $R$-module homomorphism with kernel $N$. Such $M/N$ is defined as the **quotient module**.
## Free module
Module $M$ over $R$ is $R$-**free** if it has a **basis**, i.e., a collection of elements $m_i$ satisfying:
1. **Generator**: any $m\in M$ can be written as $m=\sum r_im_i,r_i\in R$. 
2. **Linear independence**: $r_1m_1+\cdots+r_nm_n=0$ implies $r_i=0,\forall i$.
Equivalently, any element in $M$ has a **unique** $R$-linear expansion into the generators.
- **Module over division ring**: all (left) $R$-modules are free iff $R$ is a division ring.
  **Proof**: for $\Leftarrow$ the proof in [[Vector Space]] carries over since it relies on invertibility of arbitrary element. For $\Rightarrow$ 
- **Non-free module example**: given $R$ commutative and $I$ a non-trivial ideal, the quotient ring $R/I$ is not free as an $R$-module, since $i\overline r=0,\forall i\in I$, which implies $\overline r$ cannot be in the basis.
## Direct product of modules
For finite case we basically treat direct sum and product as the same, although direct sum implicitly carries a requirement for linearity (which is true in almost all cases of practice). However in infinite case an infinite direct product and infinite direct sum could be quite different.
- For a set of $R$-modules $M_1,\cdots,M_k$, we **External direct product (sum)**: defined as $M_1\times \cdots\times M_k$ with component-wise operation.
- For a set of submodules $N_1,\cdots,N_k$ of $R$-module $M$, we define **Internal direct product (sum)** $M=N_1\oplus\cdots\oplus N_k$ with the following equivalent conditions:
	1. The map $\pi:N_1\times\cdots\times N_k\to N_1+\cdots+N_k$ given by $\pi(a_1,\cdots,a_k)=\sum a_k$ is an isomorphism.
	2. $N_j\cap(\sum_{i\neq j}N_i)=\varnothing,\forall 1\le j\le k$.
	3. Every $x\in\sum N_i$ can be written uniquely in the form $\sum a_i$ where $a_i\in N_i$.
## Tensor product of modules
For a left $R$-module $N$ and right $R$-module $M$, we define the **tensor product of $M,N$ over $R$**, denoted $M\otimes_RN$, as an abelian [[Group]] together with $\otimes:M\times N\to M\otimes_R N$ that is **$R$-balanced**:  $$\begin{align}(m_1+m_2)\otimes n&=m_1\otimes n+m_2\otimes n\\m\otimes (n_1+n_2)&=m\otimes n_1+m\otimes n_2\\ mr\otimes n&=m\otimes rn\end{align}$$Each element $x=\sum_{i=1}^{<\infty} m_i\otimes n_i\in M\otimes_RN$ is called a **tensor**, with each $m_i\otimes n_i$ called a **simple tensor**.

- **Extension of scalers**: for a ring extension $R\subset S$ and a left $R$-module $N$ we take $M=S$ and define an **extension of scaler** as the tensor product $S\otimes_RN$. This structure naturally becomes a left $S$-module under the action of left multiplication.
	- Extension of scalers for free $R$-modules $N$ to the ring $S$ is $$S\otimes_RR^n\cong S^n$$This is shown by considering the free module as direct sum.
## Exact sequence
A sequence of homomorphism $$A\xrightarrow\psi B\xrightarrow\phi C$$ is said to be exact at $B$ if $\operatorname{im}\psi=\ker\phi$. Sequence of the form $0\rightarrow A\xrightarrow\psi B\xrightarrow\phi C\rightarrow0$ that is exact at $A,B,C$ is called a **short exact sequence**, and $B$ is an **extension** of $A$ by $C$.
- A **homomorphism of short exact sequences** is a tuple $(\alpha,\beta,\gamma)$ such that $A\xrightarrow\alpha A',B\xrightarrow\beta B',C\xrightarrow\gamma C'$ are module homomorphisms and the overall diagram commutes. When $\alpha,\gamma=id$ and $\beta$ is isomorphism, then $B,B'$ are said to be **equivalent extensions**.
- **Short five lemma**: for a homomorphism $(\alpha,\beta,\gamma)$, if $\alpha,\gamma$ are injective/surjective/isomorphisms, then $\beta$ is also injective/surjective/isomorphism.
## Annihilator
For a left $R$-module $M$ where $R$ is a [[Ring]], the annihilator of $N\subset M$ is defined as $$\ann_R(N)=\set{r\in R:rn=0,\forall n\in N}$$When $N=\set{n}$ we'll simply denote it as $\ann_R(n)$.
- **[[Ideal]] structure**: $\ann_R(N)$ is a left ideal of $R$. If $N\le M$ then $\ann_R(N)$ is moreover a two-sided ideal of $R$. 
  **Proof**: it's obvious that $\ann_R(N)$ is a left idea since $$rn=0\quad\Longrightarrow\quad srn=0,\quad\forall s\in R$$if we have $N\le M$, then by definition for any $r\in\ann_R(N),n\in N$ we have $$(rs)n=r(sn)=rn'=0\where n'\in N$$hence $\ann_R(N)$ is further a two-sided ideal.
	- **Annihilator in [[Polynomial Ring]]**: since any $F[X]$ given [[Field]] $F$ is a PID, we immediately know that an annihilator in any left $F[X]$-module $M$ is also a principle idea.
