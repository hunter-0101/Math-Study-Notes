A **principal ideal domain (PID)** is an [[Integral Domain]] where all [[Ideal]] are principal: $$I=(a)\text{ for some }a\in R,\quad\forall I\in\Id R$$PID is an algebraic structure that behaves like $\mathbb Z$ w.r.t. divisibility. 
- **The "valuation gap"**: while all PIDs behave like $\mathbb{Z}$ or $F[X]$ regarding ideal structure, they do not necessarily possess a Euclidean valuation. Thus, we cannot always perform a formal "division with remainder," even if GCDs always exist via ideal sums. 
## Properties
- **Algebraic structure**: we prove the position at which PID stands in the hierarchy of [[Integral Domain]] by gradually shrinking the structure class:
	- **Noetherian property**: every PID is a [[Noetherian Ring]] because every ideal is generated by a single element (and thus is finitely generated). 
	- **PID is GCD domain**: this follows immediately from the ideal intersection criterion ([[Integral Domain]]). 
	- **PID is UFD**: the existence of finite factorization follows from the Noetherian property above, and uniqueness follows from the fact that irreducible elements are prime in a PID (as shown below). 
- **Characterization of ideal via generator**: in a PID, the correspondence between arithmetic properties of elements and the lattice structure of ideals is perfect.
	- **Maximal ideal**: we have $$(m)\text{ is maximal}\qiffq m\in\irr R$$**Proof**: $(\Rightarrow)$ is trivial by contradiction; for $(\Leftarrow)$, notice that $$(m)\subsetneq(n)\subsetneq R\imply m=nd,d\notin R^\times\imply m\notin\irr R$$Which is also a contradiction. 
	- **Prime ideal**: we have $$(p)\text{ is prime}\qiffq p \text{ is prime}$$This follows directly from definition. 
- **Maximal vs. prime ideal**: in PID, and ideal is maximal iff it's prime. 
  **Proof**: by the characterization of ideal via generator and discussion in [[Integral Domain]], $$(p)\text{ is prime}\qiffq p\text{ is prime}\imply p\text{ is maximal}\imply(p)\text{ is maximal}$$By discussion in [[Ideal]] we know that any maximal ideal is prime, therefore we conclude that these two kinds of ideals are actually equivalent. 
	- **Irreducible vs. prime element**: as a corollary, we have $$p\in\irr R\qiffq p\text{ is prime}$$This is the core result we used for proving that PID is UFD. 
- **Bézout's identity**: given arbitrary $a_i\in R,1\le i\le n$ we have $$\gcd(a_1,\cdots,a_n)=d\quad\iff\quad\sum_{i=1}^n(a_i)=(d)$$**Proof**: for $(\Leftarrow)$, it's obvious by definition of [[Ideal]] that $d\mid a_i$, and for any $d'$ with the division condition $d'\mid a_i,1\le i\le n$, write $a_i=k_i'd'$, then we have $$d=\sum_{i=1}^nr_ia_i=\sum_{i=1}^nr_ik_i'd'=\left(\sum_{i=1}^nr_ik_i'\right)d'\quad\Longrightarrow\quad d'\mid d$$hence by definition ([[Ring]]) we have $d=\gcd(a_1,\cdots,a_n)$. Conversely, for $(\Rightarrow)$, since $R$ is a PID we already know RHS is well-defined, and it suffices to show that $(d)$ is unique among all GCDs, which follows naturally from the equivalence condition above. 
	- **Bézout domain**: an integral domain in which sum of two principal ideals is also principal is called a Bézout domain. Its position in the hierarchy of integral domain is $$\text{PID}\subsetneq\text{Bézout Domain}\subsetneq\text{GCD Domain}$$This is the weakest algebraic structure required for the Bézout's identity to hold for any pair of elements.
- **Lemma of Kaplansky**: an integral domain $R$ is a PID if and only if it is a UFD and its Krull dimension is at most 1 (i.e., every non-zero prime ideal is maximal).
## Examples & Counterexamples
The following examples illustrate the boundaries of the PID definition from two different angles: the failure of the PID property in higher dimensions and the existence of PIDs without Euclidean structures.
- **[[Polynomial Ring]] over $\mathbb Z$**: since $\mathbb Z$ is a Euclidean domain but not a field, $\mathbb{Z}[X]$ is a UFD (by Gauss's Lemma) but fails to be a PID.
	- **Example**: consider the ideal $I = (2, x)$. This ideal consists of all polynomials with an even constant term. If $I$ were principal, say $I = (f(x))$, then $f(x)$ must divide 2 (implying $f \in \{\pm 1, \pm 2\}$) and $f(x)$ must divide $x$ (implying $f$ is a unit or $x$). The only common divisors are $\pm 1$. But $(1) = \mathbb{Z}[x]$, and $1 \notin (2, x)$ because no combination $2 \cdot p(x) + x \cdot q(x)$ can equal 1.
	- **Conclusion:** In $\mathbb{Z}[x]$, the Krull dimension is 2 (e.g., $(0) \subsetneq (x) \subsetneq (2, x)$), which violates the PID requirement that prime ideals be maximal.
- **Ring of integers**: consider the ring of integers $$\mathcal O_K=\mathbb{Z}\sqbraket{\frac{1 + \sqrt{-19}}{2}}\where K=\mathbb Q\paren{\sqrt{-19}}$$This is a classic example of a PID that is **not** a Euclidean Domain.
	- **The Insight:** The ring is a PID because its class number is 1, meaning all its fractional ideals are principal. One can prove it is not Euclidean by showing it lacks "universal side divisors." Specifically, there is no element $u$ that can serve as a remainder for all possible divisions in a way that satisfies the Euclidean valuation requirements.
	- **Conclusion:** This demonstrates that while the "ideal-is-principal" condition provides unique factorization and Bézout identities, it does not provide the computational algorithm for division. 
## Structure theorem of finitely generated module over PID
There are two primary forms of the structure theorem. Below we assume that $M$ is a finitely generated [[Module]] over an PID $R$. 
1. **Invariant factor decomposition**: there exists a unique descending sequence of ideals $$(d_1)\supseteq(d_2)\supseteq\cdots\supseteq(d_n)\where d_i\notin R^\times,\forall i$$such that $M$ is isomorphic to the sum of cyclic modules: $$M \cong R^k \oplus\bigoplus_{i} R/(d_i)$$Here $R^k$ is the free part and $d_1 \mid d_2 \mid \dots \mid d_n$ are the **invariant factors**. 
2. **Primary decomposition**: there exists a unique set of proper primary ideals $(q_i)\neq R$ s.t. $$M\cong R^k\oplus\bigoplus_{i}R/(q_i)$$Here $q_i$ are the **elementary divisors**. 
	- **Primary ideal as power of primes**: in a PID each primary ideal is a power of primes, i.e., $$(q_i)=\paren{p_i^{r_i}}=(p_i)^{r_i},\quad\forall i$$This form is also used by some authors. 
	- **Complete decomposition**: each of $R/(q_i)$ are indecomposable, therefore the primary decomposition is actually a complete decomposition. We also conclude from this that all modules over PID are completely decomposable modules.  
**Proof**: a good reference for the proof is this [note](https://math.uchicago.edu/~may/REU2017/REUPapers/Levine.pdf)
- **Discussion**: while the invariants (rank, invariant factors, and elementary divisors) are unique, the isomorphism between $M$ and its canonical form is not unique, and does not even preserve the direct sum decomposition. 
- **Corollaries**: this theorem provides a unified framework for many useful results.
	- **Classification of finitely generated abelian group**: taking $R=\mathbb Z$ yields corresponding versions of theorems in [[Product of Group]]. 
	- **[[Jordan Normal Form]]**: taking $R = F[x]$ and considering a vector space as a $F[x]$-module via a linear operator $T$ yields the Jordan normal form, given that the underlying [[Field]] is algebraically closed. 
	- **Frobenius normal form**: invariant factor + companion matrix yields the Frobenius normal form.
