A [[Ring]] $R$ is  **(left-/right-)noetherian** if all its (left/right) [[Ideal]] satisfies the (left/right) **ascending chain condition (ACC)** - every increasing sequence of ideals has a largest element: $$I_1\subset I_2\subset\cdots\imply\exists n\in\mathbb N\ \st\ I_n=I_{n+1}=\cdots$$Equivalently, this can also be characterized by:
1. **Finite generation characterization**: $R$ is (left-/right-)Noetherian if every (left/right) ideal is finitely generated. 
   **Proof**: for $(\Leftarrow)$, contraction follows by notice that any infinitely generated ideal gives rise to an infinite ascending chain that does not stabilize by $$I=(r_1,r_2,\cdots)\imply(r_1)\subset(r_1,r_2)\subset\cdots$$For $(\Rightarrow)$, existence of infinite ascending chain that does not stabilize also gives rise to a infinitely generated ideal by $$I_1\subset I_2\subset\cdots\imply I=\bigcup_{n\in\mathbb N}I_n=(r_1,r_2,\cdots)\where r_n\in I_{n}\setminus I_{n-1}$$Here the constructed ideal is not finitely generated, otherwise there exists $N\in\mathbb N$ s.t. $r_n\in I_N,\forall n>n$, which implies that $I_n=I_N,\forall n\ge N$, a contradiction. 
2. **Ideal maximal condition**: every non-empty subset of $\Id R$ has a maximal element ([[Order]]).
   **Proof**: $(\Leftarrow)$ is trivial by applying the ideal maximal condition to an ascending ideal chain; $(\Rightarrow)$ is also trivial by constructing a infinite ascending chain of ideals that do not stabilize using the absence of maximal element. 
- **Intuition**: noetherian ring can be seen as a generalization of PID - instead of requiring all ideals to be singly generated we're relaxing the constraint to finiteness of system of generators. It's useful because the property of being finitely generated is preserved under many constructions of new rings from old rings, (e.g., Hilbert basis theorem in [[Polynomial Ring]]), while PID does not enjoy such invariance.
