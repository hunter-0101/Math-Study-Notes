For $f\in L^1(\mathbb R)$ by mimicking [[Fourier Series]] expansion (or directly apply it on $\mathbb R$ and use Euler's formula) we get $$f(x)\sim\int_\mathbb R\hat f(\xi)e^{2\pi i\xi x}d\xi,\quad \hat f(\xi)=\int_\mathbb R f(x)e^{-2\pi i\xi x}dx$$By composing two transforms together, we get the **Fourier integral** of a function.
- **Riemann-Lebesgue lemma**: let $f\in L^1(\mathbb R^n)$ be an integrable function and $\hat f$ the Fourier transform, then $|\hat f(\xi)|\to0$ as $|\xi|\to\infty$. Proof follows the standard mechanism and is similar to that in [[Fourier Series]]. Below we provide another proof in the more general framework:
  **Proof**: notice that $$\int_{\mathbb R^d}f(x)e^{-2\pi ix\xi}dx=\frac{1}{2}\int_{\mathbb R^d}(f(x)-f(x-\xi'))e^{-2\pi i\xi x}dx\quad\text{where}\quad \xi'=\frac{1}{2|\xi|}$$now let $\xi\to\infty$, then continuity under translation yields the result ([[Lebesgue Integral]]). This also helps to generalize the result to any kernel that is bounded.
- **Convergence**: if $f,\hat f\in L^1(\mathbb R)$ then $$f(x)=\int\hat f(\xi)e^{2\pi i\xi x}d\xi,\quad\ae x\in\mathbb R$$**Proof**: we have that $$I(x)=\int\hat f(\xi)e^{2\pi i\xi x}d\xi=\int\left(\int f(t)e^{-2\pi i\xi t}dt\right)e^{2\pi i\xi x}d\xi=\int\left(\int f(t)e^{2\pi i\xi(x-t)}dt\right)d\xi$$Now introduce a Gaussian factor to ensure integrability over $\mathbb R^2$: $$I_\epsilon(x)=\int\left(\int f(t)e^{-\epsilon^2\xi^2/4}e^{2\pi i\xi(x-t)}dt\right)d\xi$$by Fubini's theorem ([[Lebesgue Integral]]) we have $$I_\epsilon(x)=\int\hat f(\xi) e^{-\epsilon^2\xi^2/4}e^{2\pi i\xi x}d\xi\quad\overset{\text{DCT}}{\Longrightarrow}\quad I_\epsilon(x)\to\int\hat f(\xi)e^{2\pi i\xi x}d\xi,\quad\forall x\in\mathbb R$$on the other hand by integrating w.r.t $\xi$ first we get $$I_\epsilon(x)=\int f(t)\frac{2\sqrt\pi}{\epsilon}e^{-4\pi^2(x-t)^2/\epsilon^2}dt=f*K_\epsilon(x)\quad\text{where}\quad K_\epsilon(t)=\frac{2\sqrt\pi}{\epsilon}e^{-4\pi^2t^2/\epsilon^2}$$for which we can invoke [[Convolution#Good kernel]] to get $I_\epsilon(x)\xto{\ae}f(x),\epsilon\to0$. The above results establishes the inversion formula.
	- **Uniqueness**: if $\hat f(\xi)\equiv0$ then $f(x)=0\ae x$. Hence FT is injective.
## Properties
Below we denote the Fourier transform as $\mathcal F:L^1(\mathbb R)\to C_0(\hat{\mathbb R})$.
- **Linearity**: $\mathcal{F}[c_1f_1+c_2f_2]=c_1\mathcal{F}[f_1]+c_2\mathcal{F}[f_2],\mathcal{F}^{-1}[c_1\hat f_1+c_2\hat f_2]=c_1\mathcal{F}_{-1}[\hat f_1]+c_2\mathcal{F}^{-1}[\hat f_2]$
- **Translation**: $\mathcal{F}[f(x\pm x_0)](\xi)=\mathcal{F}[f](\xi)e^{\pm2\pi ix_0},\mathcal{F}^{-1}[\hat f(\xi\pm\xi_0)](x)=\mathcal{F}^{-1}[\hat f](x)e^{\mp2\pi ix}$
- **Differentiation**: for differentiable $f$ with $f'\in L^1(\mathbb R)$ we have $$\mathcal{F}[f']=2\pi i\xi\cdot\mathcal{F}[f]$$Proof relies on integration by parts. In general this can be applied to $f^{(n)}$ if $f^{(k)}\in L^1,\forall k\le n$.
- **Convolution theorem**: $\mathcal{F}(f_1*f_2)=\mathcal{F}f_1\cdot\mathcal{F}f_2$. This follows directly from the Fubini's theorem. [[Lebesgue Integral]] 
- **Boundedness**: $\|\hat f\|_\infty\le\|f\|_1$, by a simple triangle inequality.
- **Continuity**: $\hat f(\xi)$ is uniformly continuous on $\hat{\mathbb R}$.
  **Proof**: boundedness is obvious, for uniform continuity notice that by DCT we can take $R$ large enough such that $\int_{|x|\ge R}f<\epsilon$ for any $\epsilon>0$. Now for $\eta<\delta$ apply Riemann-Lebesgue lemma we have $$\begin{align}|\hat f(\xi+\eta)-\hat f(\xi)|&\le\int_\mathbb R|f(x)||e^{-2\pi i\eta x}-1|dx\\&=\left(\int_{|x|<R}+\int_{|x|\ge R}\right)|f(x)||e^{-2\pi i\eta x}-1|dx\\&\le\epsilon_1\|f\|_1+2\epsilon_2\end{align}$$This is sufficient for uniform continuity.
- **Upper bound**: given $f\in C^n,f^{(k)}\in L^1,\forall k\le n$ we have $\hat f(\xi)\le1/(1+|2\pi\xi|^n)$.
  **Proof**: by differentiation property we have $$\begin{align}(1+|2\pi\xi|^n)|\hat f(\xi)|&=|\hat f(\xi)|+\left|\mathcal F[f^{(n)}(x)]\right|\\&\le\|f\|_1+\|f^{(n)}\|_1\end{align}$$hence the inequality follows.
	- If we delete the condition $f^{(k)}\in L^1$ but add that $f$ is compactly supported, then by another easy estimation we can derive a similar inequality.
## Fourier transform of special functions
- **Gaussian**: from [[Normal Distribution]] we have $$f(x)=Ae^{-ax^2},\quad\hat f(\xi)=\frac{A\sqrt{\pi}}{\sqrt{a}}e^{-\pi^2\xi^2/a}$$Notice that larger $a$ corresponds to more sharply picked $f(x)$ and wider $\hat f(\xi)$. Also we may say that $f(x)=e^{-\pi x^2}$ is the most "perfect" function under Fourier transform.
- **Exponential distribution**: consider the double exponential distribution, we have $$f(x)=Ae^{-b|x|},\quad \hat f(\xi)=\frac{Ab}{\pi(b^2+4\pi^2\xi^2)}$$where $\hat f(\xi)$ is called a **Lorentzian**.
- **Delta function**: we're interested in the Fourier transform of a constant function. Notice that $$f(x)=1,\quad\hat f(\xi)=\int_\mathbb R e^{-2\pi i\xi x}dx=\delta(\xi)$$If we look at the above two examples, by taking limit we may find $$\delta(x)=\lim_{a\to 0}\frac{\exp(-\pi^2x^2/a)}{2\sqrt{\pi a}}=\lim_{b\to0}\frac{b}{\pi(b^2+4\pi^2x^2)}$$more general discussion can be found in [[Special Function Gallery#Dirac delta function]].
## FT on $L^p$
we rely on the [[Riesz Interpolation Theorem]] to define FT on any $L^p$ space.
- **Convolution theorem**: given $f\in L^p,g\in L^1$ we have $\mathcal F(f*g)=\mathcal F f\cdot\mathcal Fg$. This follows from the Fubini's theorem, guaranteed by the inequality $\|f*g\|_p\le\|f\|_p\|g\|_1$. [[Convolution]]
- **Hausdorff-Young inequality**: given $p,q$ conjugate with $p\in[1,2]$, and $f\in L^p$ we have $$\|\hat f\|_q\le\|f\|_p$$hence $\mathcal F:L^p\to L^q$ is a bounded.
