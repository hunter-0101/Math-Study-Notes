Motivated by integration by parts ([[Lebesgue Integral]]), we say $f\in L^p(\mathbb R^d)$ have **weak derivative** up to order $k$ if for any $\alpha=(\alpha_1,\cdots,\alpha_d)$ with $|\alpha|=\sum\alpha_i\le k$ there exists a $g_\alpha\in L^p_{\text{loc}}$ s.t. $$\int g_\alpha\varphi=(-1)^{|\alpha|}\int fD^\alpha\varphi,\quad\forall\varphi\in C_c^\infty(\mathbb R^d)$$We call $g_\alpha$ the $\alpha$-weak partial derivative of $f$. Obviously such $g_\alpha$ (when exists) is unique. Define the **Sobolev space** $W^{k,p}(\mathbb R^d)\subset L^p(\mathbb R^d)$ as the space consisting of functions satisfying the above property, with norm defined as $$\|f\|_{k,p}=\left(\sum_{|\alpha|\le k}\|D^\alpha f\|^p_p\right)^{1/p}\quad\text{or}\quad\|f\|_{k,p}=\sum_{|\alpha|\le k}\|D^\alpha f\|_p$$The above two norms are equivalent in the sense of norm equivalence ([[Vector Space]]). We denote $W_0^{k,p}=\overline{C_c^\infty}\cap W^{k,p}$.
