Given open $\Omega\subset\mathbb R^n$ a general Laplace's equation on $\mathbb R^n$ takes the form $$\Delta u=0,\quad u\in C^2(\Omega)$$where $\Delta$ is the Laplace operator ([[Differential Calculus on Euclidean Space]]).
- **Radial solution**: by setting $u(x)=v(r)$ we can solve that $$v''+\frac{n-1}{r}v'=0\quad\Longrightarrow\quad v(r)=\begin{cases}b\ln r+c,&n=2\\ br^{2-n},&n\ge3\end{cases}$$For this reason we define the fundamental solution of Laplace equation as $$\Phi(x)=\begin{cases}-\dfrac{1}{2\pi}\ln|x|,& n=2\\ \dfrac{1}{n(n-2)\alpha(n)}\dfrac{1}{|x|^{n-2}},& n\ge3\end{cases}$$where $\alpha(n)$ is the volume of the unit $n$-ball. [[Gamma Function]]
	- **Decay rate**: we can easily show that $$|\nabla\Phi(x)|\le\frac{C}{|x|^{n-1}},\quad|D^2\Phi(x)|=|H_\Phi|\le\frac{C}{|x|^n}$$by direct computation, where $D^2$ denotes the Hessian matrix. [[Differential Calculus on Euclidean Space]]
- **Poisson's equation**: for $f\in C_c^2(\mathbb R^n)$ define $u(x)=(\Phi*f)(x)$, then $u\in C^2(\mathbb R^n)$ with $-\Delta u=f$.
  **Proof**: by [[Convolution]] we have $u_{ij}=\Phi*f_{ij}$, hence $u\in C^2$. Now we separate $\Delta u(x)=I_\varepsilon+J_\varepsilon$ into integration over $B(0,\varepsilon)$ and $\mathbb R^n-B(0,\varepsilon)$, then $$|I_\varepsilon|=\left|\int_{B(0,\varepsilon)}\Phi(y)\Delta_xf(x-y)dy\right|\le C\|H_f\|_\infty\int_{B(0,\varepsilon)}|\Phi(y)|dy\le\begin{cases}C\varepsilon^2|\ln\varepsilon|,&n=2\\C\varepsilon^2,&n\ge3\end{cases}$$Meanwhile, notice that $\Delta_xf(x-y)=\Delta_y(x-y)$, further by integration by parts ([[Integral Calculus on Euclidean Space]]) we have $$\begin{align}J_\varepsilon&=\int_{\mathbb R^n-B(0,\varepsilon)}\Phi(y)\Delta_yf(x-y)dy\\&=-\int_{\mathbb R^n-B(0,\varepsilon)}\nabla\Phi(y)\cdot\nabla_yf(x-y)dy+\int_{\partial B(0,\varepsilon)}\Phi(y)\frac{\partial f}{\partial\vec v}(x-y)dS(y)\\&=:K_\varepsilon+L_\varepsilon\end{align}$$where $\vec v$ is the inward pointing unit normal vector along $\partial B(0,\varepsilon)$. Now that $$|L_\varepsilon|\le\|\nabla f\|_\infty\int_{\partial B(0,\varepsilon)}|\Phi(y)|dS(y)\le\begin{cases}C\varepsilon|\ln\varepsilon|,&n=2\\C\varepsilon,&n\ge3\end{cases}$$while again by integration by parts applied on $K_\varepsilon$ we have $$\begin{align}K_\varepsilon&=\int_{\mathbb R^n-B(0,\varepsilon)}\Delta\Phi(y)f(x-y)dy-\int_{\partial B(0,\varepsilon)}\frac{\partial\Phi}{\partial\vec v}(y)f(x-y)dS(y)\\&=-\int_{\partial B(0,\varepsilon)}\frac{\partial\Phi}{\partial\vec v}(y)f(x-y)dS(y)&\text{Since }\Delta\Phi=0,\forall x\neq0\\&=-\frac{1}{n\alpha(n)\varepsilon^{n-1}}\int_{\partial B(0,\varepsilon)}f(x-y)dS(y)&\text{Direct calculation}\\&=-\frac{1}{\lambda(\partial B(x,\varepsilon))}\int_{\partial B(x,\varepsilon)}f(y)dS(y)\to-f(x),\quad\varepsilon\to0\end{align}$$All results above, combined, completes the proof.
	- **Discussion**: we cannot apply [[Differentiation Under the Integral Sign]] to get $\Delta u=0$ since $D^2\Phi$ is not locally integrable near $0$ by the estimation above. Actually $-\Delta\Phi=\delta_0$ the delta function ([[Special Function Gallery]]), and formally we indeed have $$-\Delta u(x)=-\int_{\mathbb R^n}\Delta\Phi(x-y)f(y)dy=\int_{\mathbb R^n}\delta(x-y)f(y)dy=f(x)$$this is valid in the [[distribution]] sense.
