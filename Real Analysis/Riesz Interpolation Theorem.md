**Riesz interpolation theorem**: given $1\le p_0,p_1,q_0,q_1\le\infty$ and $T:L^{p_0}+L^{p_1}\to L^{q_0}+L^{q_1}$, if $$\|T\|_{p_0\to q_0}\le M_0,\quad\|T\|_{p_1\to q_1}\le M_1$$then for any pair of indexes $p,q$ with $$\frac{1}{p}=\frac{1-t}{p_0}+\frac{t}{p_1},\quad\frac{1}{q}=\frac{1-t}{q_0}+\frac{t}{q_1},\quad t\in[0,1]$$we have $\|T\|_{p\to q}\le M$ where $M\le M_0^{1-t}M_1^t$.
**Proof**: we start with simple functions $f$ with $\|f\|_p=1$. By equality condition of Hölder's inequality it suffices to show that $$\left|\int(Tf)gd\mu\right|\le M\|Tf\|_q\|g\|_{q^*},\quad\forall \|g\|_{q^*}=1,\frac{1}{q}+\frac{1}{q^*}=1$$Assume that $p<\infty,q>1$, and let $$f_z=|f|^{\gamma(z)}\frac{f}{|f|},\quad\gamma(z)=p\left(\frac{1-z}{p_0}+\frac{z}{p_1}\right);\quad g_z=|g|^{\delta(z)}\frac{g}{|g|},\quad\delta(z)=q^*\left(\frac{1-z}{q_0^*}+\frac{z}{q_1^*}\right)$$Notice that $f_t=f$, with $\|f_z\|_{p_0}=1,\forall\Re z=0$ and $\|f_z\|_{q_1}=1,\forall\Re z=1$, and similarly for $g_z$. Let $$\Phi(z)=\int(Tf_z)g_zd\mu$$by the assumption that $f,g$ are simple we can verify directly that $\Phi\in\mathcal O(S)$ and verifies the conditions in the three-lines lemma, hence the result follows. For general $f$ by density of simple functions it suffices to show that $Tf_n\xto\ae Tf$ if $f_n\xto{L^p} f$, which can be done by convergence acceleration ([[Subsequence Acceleration]]). Finally for $p=\infty$ the result follows directly from Hölder's inequality, while for $q=1$ we take $g_z=g,\forall z\in S$ and argue as in $q>1$. 
- **Three-lines lemma**: given $\Phi(z)\in\mathcal O(S)\cap C_b(\overline S)$ where $S=\set{0<\Re z<1}$, denote $$M_0=\sup_{y\in\mathbb R}|\Phi(iy)|,\quad M_1=\sup_{y\in\mathbb R}|\Phi(1+iy)|$$then we have $$\sup_{y\in\mathbb R}|\Phi(t+iy)|\le M_0^{1-t}M_1^t,\quad\forall 0\le t\le1$$**Proof**: assume first that $M_0=M_1=1$ and $\Phi$ vanishes at $\infty$. Pick a sequence $z_n\in S$ s.t. $|\Phi(z_n)|\to\sup_S|\Phi|$, then by vanishing assumption $z_n$ has a limit point $z_0$, and by maximum modulus principle ([[Complex Geometry]]) we must have $z_0\in\partial S$, which means $\sup|\Phi|\le1$. If we remove the vanishing assumption we can consider $$\Phi_\varepsilon(z)=\Phi(z)\exp(\varepsilon(z^2-1)),\quad\varepsilon>0$$It can be verified that $\Phi_\varepsilon$ satisfies the vanishing assumption, hence $|\Phi_{\varepsilon}|\le1$, and by taking $\varepsilon\to0$ we get $|\Phi|\le1$. Now for arbitrary $M_0,M_1$ we can consider $\tilde\Phi(z)=M_0^{z-1}M_1^{-z}\Phi(z)$ to generalize the above result. This is essentially [[Domain-Range Regularization]].
- **Riesz convexity theorem**: define the **Riesz diagram** of $T$ a subset of $[0,1]\times[0,1]$ consisting all points $(x,y)$ s.t. $T\in\mathcal B(L^p,L^q)$ where $x=1/p,y=1/q$. Denote $M_{x,y}$ the optimal coefficient, then by Riesz interpolation theorem we know that the Riesz diagram of $T$ is a convex set, and $\ln M_{x,y}$ is a convex function on the set.
## Application
- **Hausdorff-Young inequality**: denote $a_n$ the [[Fourier Series]] coefficient of $f$ on $[0,2\pi]$, then $$\left(\sum|a_n|^q\right)^{1/q}\le\left(\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}|f(\theta)|^p\right)^{1/p},\quad \left(\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}|f(\theta)|^q\right)^{1/q}\le\left(\sum|a_n|^p\right)^{1/p}$$both valid for $1\le p\le2,1/p+1/q=1$. The case $p=q=2$ is exactly the Parseval's identity, while $p=1,q=\infty$ or $p=\infty,q=1$ are easily verified.
	- **Fourier transform analogy**: by Riesz interpolation theorem applied on [[FT on L1]] and [[FT on L2]] we know that $\mathcal F$ has a unique extension to $\mathcal F\in\mathcal B(L^p,L^q)$ where $p,q$ are described above, with $\|\mathcal Ff\|_q\le\|f\|_p$.
- **Young's convolution inequality**: the result described in [[Lp Space]] can also be proved in the following fashion: fix $g$ and let $Tf=f*g$, then Young's inequality ([[Convolution]]) implies that $\|Tf\|_r\le M\|f\|_1$ where $M=\|g\|_r$, while Hölder's inequality implies $\|Tf\|_\infty\le M\|f\|_{r^*}$, hence RIT yields the result.
