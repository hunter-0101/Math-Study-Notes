For a left $R$-[[Module]] $M$ where $R$ is a [[Ring]], the annihilator of subset $N\subset M$ is defined as $$\ann_R(S)=\set{r\in R:rs=0,\forall s\in S}$$The annihilator of one single element is denote $\ann_R(s)$.
- **Primary motivation:** the annihilator measures the "obstruction" to a module being free. In the study of finitely generated modules over a PID, the annihilator (specifically the generator of the ideal) determines the torsion structure. 
- **Relation to cyclic modules:** For any $m \in M$, there is an isomorphism of left $R$-modules: $$Rm \cong R/\ann_R(m)$$This identifies the structure of the cyclic submodule generated by $m$ entirely with the coset structure of its annihilator in $R$. 
- **Annihilators as kernels of action**: the annihilator can be viewed as the kernel of the action representation $\rho: R \to \text{End}_{\mathbb{Z}}(M)$. 
	- **Example 1 (Torsion in $\mathbb{Z}$-modules):** Let $M = \mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z}$ as a $\mathbb{Z}$-module. The annihilator of the element $m = (1, 1)$ is $12\mathbb{Z}$ (the LCM of the orders), illustrating how the annihilator captures the "least common" scalar that nullifies a set of elements.
	- **Example 2 (Linear Operators):** Let $V$ be a $k$-vector space and $T: V \to V$ a linear operator. We view $V$ as a $k[x]$-module where $f(x) \cdot v = f(T)(v)$. The annihilator $\ann_{k[x]}(V)$ is the ideal generated by the **minimal polynomial** of $T$. If we consider a specific vector $v$, $\ann_{k[x]}(v)$ is generated by the $T$-annihilator of $v$, reflecting the local-to-global structure of the operator.
## Properties
- **[[Ideal]] structure**: $\ann_R(N)$ is a left ideal of $R$. If $N\le M$ then $\ann_R(N)$ is moreover a two-sided ideal of $R$. 
  **Proof**: it's obvious that $\ann_R(N)$ is a left idea since $$rn=0\quad\Longrightarrow\quad srn=0,\quad\forall s\in R$$if we have $N\le M$, then by definition for any $r\in\ann_R(N),n\in N$ we have $$(rs)n=r(sn)=rn'=0\where n'\in N$$hence $\ann_R(N)$ is further a two-sided ideal.
	- **Annihilator in [[Polynomial Ring]]**: since any $F[X]$ given [[Field]] $F$ is a PID, we immediately know that an annihilator in any left $F[X]$-module $M$ is also a principle idea.
- **Inclusion-Reversing Relation (Galois Connection)**: the mapping $$\mathcal P(M)\to\Id R:S \mapsto \ann_R(S)$$ is **inclusion-reversing**. For any subsets $S_1, S_2 \subseteq M$: $$S_1 \subseteq S_2 \imply \ann_R(S_2) \subseteq \ann_R(S_1)$$This establishes a correspondence between the lattice of submodules $\mathcal{L}(M)$ and the lattice of left ideals of $R$.
	- _Note:_ This is one half of a Galois connection. The other half is the mapping of an ideal $I \subseteq R$ to its "null-set" in $M$, denoted $\ann_M(I) = \{ m \in M \mid Im = 0 \}$.
- **Sums & intersection**: for a family of submodules $\set{N_i}_{i \in I}\subseteq\sub M$ we have $$\begin{align}\ann_R\paren{\sum_{i \in I} N_i} &= \bigcap_{i \in I} \ann_R(N_i)\\\ann_R\paren{\bigcap_{i \in I} N_i} &\supseteq \sum_{i \in I} \ann_R(N_i)\end{align}$$The equality in (2) generally fails. A standard counter-example involves $R = \mathbb{Z}$ and $M = \mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z}/p^2\mathbb{Z}$.
- **Relation to module homomorphisms**: if $f: M \to N$ is an $R$-homomorphism, then $$\ann_R(S) \subseteq \ann_R(f(S)),\quad\forall S\subseteq M$$If $f$ is further an isomorphism, then $\ann_R(S) = \ann_R(f(S))$.
	- _Practical Tool:_ To prove two modules are not isomorphic, it often suffices to compare the annihilators of their elements (e.g., comparing the sets of possible orders in abelian groups).
- **Faithfulness**: $M$ is called faithful if $\ann_R(M) = \{0\}$.
	- **Identification of base ring for faithfulness**: any $R$-module $M$ can be identified as a faithful $R/\ann_R(M)$-module. This allows for the "reduction" of the base ring to one that acts effectively on the module.
- **Annihilator of the Quotient:** For a submodule $N \subseteq M$, the annihilator $\ann_R(M/N)$ consists of elements $r \in R$ such that $rM \subseteq N$. 
> **Theoretical Tool: The Double Annihilator Condition**
> 
> In certain "nice" modules (like finite-dimensional vector spaces or certain injective modules), we have a duality where $\ann_M(\ann_R(N)) = N$. This is a powerful property used in the study of Artinian rings and semi-simple modules to recover submodules from their ideal-theoretic descriptions.
