**$m$-manifold** is a second countable Hausdorff space $M$ that is locally Euclidean of dimension $m$.
- **Discussion on dimension**: manifold dimension is about its **local Euclidean structure** rather than cardinality, measure, density, etc.. The key is that it's locally indistinguishable from $\mathbb R^m$, while it's acceptable that $M$ has some "flaws", e.g.,  $\gl(n,\mathbb R)$ as a proper, dense subset of $M_n(\mathbb R)$ ([[Group Gallery]]).
## Locally Euclidean topological space
A topological space $M$ is locally Euclidean of dimension $m$ if each $p\in M$ has a neighborhood $U$ with a **homeomorphism** ([[Continuous Function]]) $\phi:U\to\mathbb R^m$. In this case we say $\dim M=m$. The pair $(U,\phi)$ is called a **chart** with $U$ the **coordinate neighborhood** and $\phi$ the **coordinate system**. The chart is centered at $p$ if $\phi(p)=0$.
- **Coordinate form of chart**: we can always find $\phi=(x^1,\cdots,x^m)$ that explicitly describes the image of each point in $M$. It's worth noting that chart is coordinate system is defined **up to diffeomorphism**, hence when talking about a coordinate system we're actually talking about the entire equivalence class.
- **Compatibility**: two charts $(U,\phi),(V,\psi)$ are $C^\infty$-compatible if the **transition functions** $$\phi\circ\psi^{-1}:\psi(U\cap V)\to\phi(U\cap V),\quad\psi\circ\phi^{-1}:\phi(U\cap V)\to\psi(U\cap V)$$are $C^\infty$. It's obvious that compatibility is reflexive and symmetric, but **not transitive**, since for $U\leftrightarrow V\leftrightarrow W$ we could say nothing about $(U\cap W)\backslash V$.
	- **Intuition**: it's natural to define differentiability on manifolds via a transition map, i.e., by its proxy on $\mathbb R^m\to\mathbb R^n$.
- **Atlas**: a $C^\infty$ atlas on a locally Euclidean space $M$ is a collection $$\mathfrak{A}=\set{(U{\alpha,\phi_\alpha})}$$ of pairwise $C^\infty$ compatible charts that cover $M$. 
	- **Transitivity of compatibility via atlas**: if both $(U,\phi),(V,\psi)$ are compatible with $\mathfrak A=\set{(W_\alpha,\sigma_\alpha)}$ then $U,V$ are compatible.

It's obvious by Zorn's lemma ([[Set]]) that any atlas is contained in a **unique maximal atlas**, by adjoining all $(U,\phi)$ that is compatible with $\mathfrak A$ into it makes it into a larger atlas.
## Manifold with boundary
Denote $\mathcal H^m=\set{(x^1,\cdots,x^m)\in\mathbb R^m:x^m\ge0}$ the upper half space. For function $f$ on $S\subset\mathbb R^m$ we say that it's continuous at $p\in S$ if there exists neighborhood $U\subset\mathbb R^m$ of $p$ and continuous function $\tilde f:U\to\mathbb R^n$ s.t. $\tilde f=f$ on $S\cap U$.
A **topological m-manifold with boundary** is a second countable, Hausdorff topological space that is locally $\mathcal H^m$. Definitions of things like charts, etc. are essentially the same as above.
- **Boundary**: an interior point of $M$ is $p\in M$ whose image under coordinate chart is interior of $\mathcal H^m$, and similarly we define boundary point of $M$. We denote the (manifold) boundary of $M$ as $\partial M$.
	- **Difference from topological boundary**: in [[Topological Space]] a boundary point is defined to be any point that is neither interior or exterior, while topologically we require $\partial M\subset M$, e.g., for $M=\{x\in\mathbb R^2:\|x\|<1\}$ its topological boundary is the unit circle $\{\|x\|=1\}$ while its manifold boundary is $\varnothing$.
	- **Manifold structure of boundary**: given a atlas $\set{(U_\alpha,\phi_\alpha)}$ of $M$ it's easy to show that $$\set{(U_\alpha\cap\partial M,\phi_\alpha|_{U_\alpha\cap\partial M})},\quad\phi_\alpha|_{U_\alpha\cap\partial M}:U_\alpha\cap\partial M\to\partial\mathcal H^m=\mathbb R^{m-1}$$forms an atlas for $\partial M$. Hence $\dim\partial M=\dim M-1$.
## Other info
- **Partition of unity**: let $\set{U_k}_{1\le k\le n}$ be a finite set of index open covering of $X$. An indexed family of continuous functions $\phi_k:X\to[0,1]$ is said to be a partition of unity subordinate to $\set{U_k}$ if $\supp\phi_k\subset U_k$ and $\sum\phi_k(x)=1$ for all $x\in X$.
	- **Existence of finite partition of unity**: let $\set{U_k}$ be a finite open covering of normal $X$, then there exists a partition of unity subordinate to $\set{U_k}$.
	  **Proof**: by normality we can choose a set $V_k$ s.t. $\overline{V_k}\subset U_k$ and replacing any number of $U_k$ by $V_k$ does not influence the covering property. Repeat this to get another $\set{W_k}$, and using Urysohn lemma ([[Separation Axioms]]) we can find continuous $\psi_k:X\to[0,1]$ s.t. $\psi_k(\overline{W_k})=1,\psi_k(X-V_k)=0$. Since $\set{W_k}$ covers $X$, $\Psi=\sum\psi_k$ is positive everywhere, and now the function $\phi_k=\psi_k/\Psi$ would be partition of unity.
- **Imbedding of manifold**: any compact $m$-manifold $X$ can be imbedded in $\mathbb R^N$ for some $N\in\mathbb N$.
  **Proof**: let $\set{U_k}_{1\le k\le n}$ be a finite open covering of $X$, each of which can be imbedded in $\mathbb R^m$ via $g_k:U_k\to\mathbb R^m$. Since $X$ is compact and Hausdorff, $X$ is normal. Let $\phi_k$ be a partition of unity dominated by $\set{U_k}$, and let $A_k=\supp\phi_k$. Define $h_k:X\to\mathbb R^m$ by $$h_k(x)=\begin{cases}\phi_k(x)\cdot g_k(x),&x\in U_k\\0=(0,\cdots,0),&x\in X-A_k\end{cases}$$then $h_k$ is well-defined (since two definitions agree on domain intersection) and continuous. Now define $F:X\to\mathbb R^n\times(\mathbb R^m)^n$ by $F(x)=(\phi_1(x),\cdots,\phi_n(x),h_1(x),\cdots,h_n(x))$, then $F$ is continuous, and it's an imbedding since it's injective (notice that $X$ is compact).
