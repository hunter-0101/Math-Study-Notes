For a [[Vector Space]] $V$ over [[Field]] $F$, the **algebraic dual space** is defined as the set of all linear functionals on $V$: $$V^\#=\mathcal L(V,F)=\set{ \ell: V \to F : \ell \text{ linear}}$$Here we're using $\#$ as the superscript to distinguish it from the [[Topological Dual Space]]. Any $\ell\in V^\#$ is called a **linear functional**. 
## Dual basis
Given $\{e_i\}_{i \in I}$ a Hamel basis of $V$ ([[Vector Space]]) we can construct its **dual basis** by $$\set{\phi^i}_{i \in I} \subset V^{\#}\quad\st\quad\phi^j(e_i)=\delta_i^j,\quad\forall i,j\in I$$Its existence and uniqueness are trivial. 
- **Relation with algebraic dual space**: in general we have that $$V^\#=\span_F(\phi^i:i\in I)\qiffq \dim V<\infty$$**Proof**: $(\Leftarrow)$ is trivial by [[Vector Space]]; for $(\Rightarrow)$, by Cantor's theorem ([[Cardinality]]) we have that $$\abs{V^\#}=|F|^{|I|}\ge2^{|I|}>|I|$$Therefore $V^\#$ is strictly larger than the space spanned by the dual basis. 
- **Dual basis projection**: for $W=\span_F(e_i:1\le i\le n)$ we pick the dual basis $\phi^i$, then we can define a (canonical) projection onto $W$ by $$P_W(v)=\sum_{i=1}^n\phi^i(v)e_i$$It can be verified that this is indeed a projection. 
	- **Dependency on the basis**: projection essentially realizes the direct sum decomposition $$V=W\oplus K\where K=\bigcap_{i=1}^n\ker\phi^i$$Since the complement $K$ is implicitly determined by $\phi^i$, which depends totally on $e_i$, we know that the constructed projection is dependent on the chosen basis. To ensure its uniqueness we have to add in other constraints, like [[Hilbert Space]] structure and orthogonality of the projection. 
- **Finite biorthogonal systems**: $\set{\phi^j}_{j=1}^n \subset V^{\#}$ that are linearly independent, we can construct a pre-dual set of vectors $\set{e_i}_{i=1}^n \subset V$ satisfying $\phi^j(e_i) = \delta_i^j$, as proved below.
    - **Structure theorem**: for the biorthogonal system defined above, we have $$V=\left(\bigcap_{1\le j\le n}\ker\phi^j\right)\oplus\operatorname{span}_F(e_i:1\le i\le n)$$**Proof**: for existence is suffices to pick $$e_i\in\left.\bigcap_{j\neq i}\ker\phi^j\right/\ker\phi^i\quad\Longrightarrow\quad\phi^j\left(\frac{e_i}{\phi^i(e_i)}\right)=\frac{\phi^j(e_i)}{\phi^i(e_i)}=\delta_i^j$$Such a vector exists, otherwise $\bigcap_{j \neq i} \ker \phi^j \subset \ker \phi^i$, which implies linear dependence (see below). The direct sum decomposition follows by verifying that $$v=\left(v-\sum_{i=1}^n\phi^i(v)e_i\right)+\sum_{i=1}^n\phi^i(v)e_i\in\left(\bigcap_{1\le j\le n}\ker\phi^j\right)+\span_F(e_i:1\le i\le n),\quad\forall v\in V$$and the sum is indeed direct since two subspaces do not intersect.
## Linear Dependence of Functionals
A set of functionals $\{\ell_i\}_{i=1}^n$ is **linearly dependent** if $$\sum_{i=1}^n\alpha_i\ell_i=0\where\alpha_i\in F$$where the scalers are non-trivial, and **linearly independent** otherwise.
* **Linear span of functionals**: given $\set{\ell_i}_{i=1}^n \subset V^{\#}$ and $\ell \in V^{\#}$ we have $$\bigcap_{i=1}^n\ker \ell_i\subset\ker\ell\quad\iff\quad\ell\in \operatorname{span}_F(\ell_1, \dots, \ell_n)$$**Proof**: $(\Leftarrow)$ is trivial. For $(\Rightarrow)$, if $\ell_1=0$, then $$\ker\ell_1=V\imply\ker\ell=V\imply\ell=0$$Otherwise we can always pick $$\ell_1(v_1)=1\imply\ell_1(v-\ell_1(v)v_1)=0\imply v-\ell_1(v)v_1\in\ker\ell_1\subset\ker\ell$$Therefore by applying $\ell$ on LHS we get $$\ell(v-\ell_1(v)v_1)=0\qiffq\ell(v)=\ell(v_1)\ell(v)$$For the induction step, applying the induction hypothesis on $\ell_i|_{\ker\ell_{n+1}},\ell|_{\ker\ell_{n+1}}$ we get $$\ell|_{\ker\ell_{n+1}}=\sum_{i=1}^n\lambda_i\ell_i|_{\ker\ell_{n+1}}\imply\ker\ell_{n+1}\subset\ker\left(\ell-\sum_{i=1}^n\lambda_i\ell_i\right)$$for which we can invoke the $n=1$ case again to complete the induction.

	  Concretely, consider the linear operator $$T:V\to F^n,\quad T(v)=(\ell_1(v),\cdots,\ell_n(v))$$which induces $\tilde T:V/\ker T\to\im T$ by the first isomorphism theorem ([[Group Homomorphism]]). Meanwhile, since we have the inclusion $$\ker T=\bigcap\ker \ell_i\subset\ker\ell$$we can also induce another linear operator with pullback $$\tilde\ell:V/\ker\ell\to\im\ell= F,\quad \hat\lambda=\tilde \ell\circ\pi_{V/\ker\ell}^{V/\ker T}\circ\tilde T^{-1}:\im T\to F$$where $\pi_{V/\ker\ell}^{V/\ker T}$ is the natural projection from $V/\ker T$ to $V/\ker\ell$. By Hahn-Banach theorem (or use a simple [[Matrix]] argument via [[Vector Space]]) we can extend $\hat\lambda$ to $\lambda:F^n\to F$ with $$\lambda\circ T=\lambda|_{\im T}\circ T=\hat\lambda\circ T=\tilde\ell\circ\pi_{V/\ker\ell}^{V/\ker T}\circ \tilde T^{-1}\circ T=\tilde\ell\circ \pi_{V/\ker\ell}^{V/\ker T}\circ\pi_{V/\ker T}^V=\ell$$Meanwhile, by RRT ([[Hilbert Space]]) applied on $(F^n,\braket{\cdot,\cdot})$ there exists $\alpha=(\alpha_1,\cdots,\alpha_n)^T$ s.t. $$\lambda=\braket{\alpha,\cdot}\quad\Longrightarrow\quad\ell(v)=\lambda\circ T(v)=\sum_{i=1}^n\alpha_i\ell_i(v),\quad\forall v\in V$$which completes the proof. 
	- **Intuition**: as discussed in [[Hyperplane]], a functional is determined totally by its kernel and uniting vector. Once we're given $\ell_i,1\le i\le n$ the condition $\bigcap\ker\ell_i\subset\ker\ell$ immediately restricts $\ell$ to those whose kernel hyperplane contains the intersection, hence the linear dependency is trivial. Note that the statement is much more intuitive under the [[Hilbert Space]] setting. By RRT we must have $$\ell_i=\braket{\cdot,v_i},\quad1\le i\le n,\quad\text{and}\quad\ell=\braket{\cdot,v}$$and it can be easily verify that $$\bigcap_{1\le i\le n}\ker\ell_i\subset\ker\ell\quad\iff\quad v\in\span_F(v_i:1\le i\le n)\quad\iff\quad\ell\in\span_F(\ell_i:1\le i\le n)$$hence the property is essentially a restatement for linear span of vectors.
	- A counterpart of this problem in the sense that, some evaluations can always be found to be linearly independent given trivial intersection of kernels, can be found in [[Sampling & Discretization]].
- **Applications**
	- **Codimension of subspace**: for a subspace $W\subset V$ we have that $$\codim W=n\quad\iff\quad W=\bigcap_{1\le i\le n}\ker\ell_i,\quad\ell_i\in V^\#$$the result above gives the converse description: if a functional $\ell$ vanishes on $\bigcap_{1\le i\le n}\ker\ell_i$, then it's a linear combination of those $\ell_i$/
	- **Lagrange multiplier**: if we're optimizing a smooth functional $f$ subject to $n$ smooth linear constraints $g_i(v)=0$ ($Dg_i$ are continuous linear functionals), at a constrained critical point we must have $$\operatorname{int}\bigcap_{1\le i\le n}\ker Dg\subset\ker Df\quad\iff\quad Df\in\span(Dg_i:1\le i\le n)$$this generalizes the previous result in [[Differential Calculus on Euclidean Space]] to any Banach space (or arbitrary [[Vector Space]]).
## Analytic Hahn-Banach Theorem
This is a purely algebraic result relying on convexity, and does not involve any topological property. Its geometric version, on the other hand, relies on the topology induced by norm, and is discussed in [[Topological Dual Space]].
**Hahn-Banach theorem**: given a seminorm ([[Normed Vector Space]]) $p$ on $V$, a linear subspace $V_0\subset V$, and $\ell_0\in V_0^\#$ s.t. $$|\ell_0(v_0)|\le p(v_0),\quad\forall v_0\in V_0$$then $\ell_0$ can be extended to $\ell$ that verifies the above inequality on $V$.
**Proof**: first consider $\ell:V\to\mathbb R$. For any $v_1\notin V_0$ denote $V_1=\span(V_0,v_1)$, then we can extend $\ell_0$ to $\ell_1$ on $V_1$ that verifies the inequality since it only requires $$-p(-v_1+w')+\ell_0(w')\le\ell_1(v_1)\le p(v_1+w)-\ell_0(w),\quad\forall w,w'\in V_0$$such $\ell_1(v_1)$ can be chosen because $\text{LHS}\le0,\text{RHS}\ge0$ by assumption and non-negativity of seminorm. At this point we let $$\mathcal V=\set{V_0\subset V_1\subset V:\text{an extension of }\ell_0\text{ satisfying all above requirements exists}}$$then $(\mathcal V,\subset)$ is a poset ([[Order]]), hence a simple application of Zorn's lemma yields the result. For complex functional we first apply the above results to $\Re\ell_0$ (regarding $V$ a linear space over $\mathbb R$) to get $\Re\ell$, and then construct $\ell$ via $$\ell(v)=\Re\ell(v)-i\Re\ell(iv)$$By linearity $\ell$ extends $\ell_0$ and verifies the desired inequality.
- **Bijection between $V^\#$ over $\mathbb R/\mathbb C$**: there is a bijection between dominated functionals in  $V^\#(\mathbb R)$ and $V^\#(\mathbb C)$ given by the above expression, since $$\ell_\mathbb C(1)=c\iff\begin{cases}\ell_\mathbb R(1)=\Re c\\\ell_\mathbb R(i)=-\Im c\end{cases}$$We can of course exchange real and imaginary part to get another bijection.
