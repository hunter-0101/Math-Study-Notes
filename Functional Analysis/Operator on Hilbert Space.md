An **operator** $T:\mathcal H_1\to\mathcal H_2$ is a linear transform ([[Vector Space]]) from one [[Hilbert Space]] to another. We denote the set of linear operators as $\mathcal L(\mathcal H_1,\mathcal H_2)$.
- **Trace**: given $\mathcal H$ separable the trace for $T\in\mathcal L(\mathcal H)$ is given by $$\tr T=\sum_i\braket{Te_i,e_i}$$where $\set{e_i}$ is an orthonormal basis. 
- **Convergence**: given $\set{T_n}\subset\mathcal B(\mathcal H)$ there are many different notions of convergence:
	- **Norm convergence**: $\|T_n-T\|\to0$.
	- **Strong convergence**: $T_nu\to Tu,\forall u\in\mathcal H$. This is a bit weaker considering $T_n=P_{e_n}$ and $T=0$.
		- For any $T\in\mathcal B(\mathcal H)$ there exists $\set{T_n}\subset\mathcal R(\mathcal H)$ that converges strongly to $T$.
	- **Weak convergence**: $\braket{T_n u,v}\to\braket{Tu,v},\forall u,v\in\mathcal H$. This is the weakest considering $T_n=R^n$ where $R$ is the right shift on $\ell^2(\mathbb N)$ and $T=0$. Also see weak convergence in [[Hilbert Space]].
- **Operator extension**: given a dense subspace $\mathcal S\subset\mathcal H$ and $T\in\mathcal B(\mathcal S)$, then $T$ extends uniquely to $\tilde T\in\mathcal B(\mathcal H)$ with $\|\tilde T\|=\|T\|$.
  **Proof**: for any $u\in\mathcal H$ we pick $u_n\in\mathcal S,u_n\to u$, and define $$\tilde Tu=\lim_{n\to\infty}Tu_n$$the limit is well-defined since: 
  - **Existence & Uniqueness**: $u_n$ is a Cauchy sequence, hence so is $Tu_n$, while $\mathcal H$ is complete. 
  - **Independence of $\set{u_n}$**: for $u_n,v_n\to u$ we have $\lim Tu_n-\lim Tv_n=\lim T(u_n-v_n)=0$.
  Then obviously $T\in\mathcal L(\mathcal H)$ by linearity of limit, and finally $T\in\mathcal B(\mathcal H)$ since $$\|\tilde Tu\|=\|\lim Tu_n\|=\lim\|Tu_n\|\le\lim\|T\|\cdot\|u_n\|=\|T\|\cdot\|u\|$$The second and third equality is due to continuity of norm ([[Vector Space]]). Norm equivalence is obvious since $\|\tilde T\|\ge\|T\|$ being an extension. 
	- **Generalization**: given $T\in\mathcal B(\mathcal S,\mathcal H_2)$ and dense $\mathcal S\subset\mathcal H_1$ there exists a unique extension $\tilde T\in\mathcal B(\mathcal H_1,\mathcal H_2)$, as illustrated below.

## Common operators
- **Orthogonal operator**: $\set{T_n}\subset\mathcal B(\mathcal H)$ is orthogonal if $T_k^*T_j=T_jT_k^*=0,\forall k\neq j$. For such set of operators we have $\im T_k\perp\im T_j$.
	- Denote $S_N=\sum_{n=0}^N T_n$ , then $S_N\to T$ strongly with $\|T\|=\sup\|T_n\|$. We only need to consider $S'=(\bigoplus\overline{\im T_n})^\perp$ (this is well-defined by [[Hilbert Space#Closed subspace]]) and do some estimation. Intuitively we can view $S_n=\diag(T_1,\cdots,T_n)$.
	- **Cotlar-Stein almost orthogonality lemma**: if $\|T_jT_k^*\|=a_{jk},\|T_j^*T_k\|=b_{jk}$, denote $$\sup_j\sum_k\sqrt{a_{jk}}=A<\infty,\quad\sup_j\sum_k\sqrt{b_{jk}}=B<\infty$$then $S_n=\sum_{k=1}^nT_k\to T$ satisfies $\|T\|\le\sqrt{AB}$.
	  **Proof**: notice that $\|T\|^2=\|T^*T\|=\|TT^*\|$, we can already establish $\|T_k\|\le\min(A,B)$. Iterate the identity yields $\|T\|^{2N}=\|(TT^*)^N\|$, hence for $S_n$ by triangle inequality we have $$\|S_n\|^{2N}=\|(S_nS_n^*)^N\|\le\sum_{1\le i_1,j_1,\cdots,i_N,j_N\le n}\|T_{i_1}T_{j_1}^*\cdots T_{i_N}T_{j_N}^*\|$$Each term in the summation can be bounded by $a_{i_1j_1}\cdots a_{i_Nj_N}$ (pairwise grouping) or $b_{j_1i_2}\cdots b_{j_{N-1}i_N}\|T_{i_1}\|\|T_{j_N}\|$ (shifted right), so if we take the geometric mean of two upper bounds, we'd get $$\|S_n\|^{2N}\le\sum_{1\le i_1,j_1,\cdots,i_N,j_N\le n} \sqrt{a_{i_1j_1}b_{j_1i_2}\cdots b_{j_{N-1}i_N}a_{i_Nj_N}\|T_{i_1}\|\|T_{j_N}\|}\le n(AB)^N$$Taking $N\to\infty$ yields $\|S_n\|\le\sqrt{AB}$, and again taking $n\to\infty$ yields the result. 
		- **Application**: the lemma is usually used in cases where typical decomposition (e.g., FT) of an operator is not well-behaved, and that different basis operators do not affect each other too much.
- **Adjoint**: for $T\in\mathcal B(\mathcal H)$ there exists a unique $T^*\in\mathcal B(\mathcal H)$ called adjoint of $A$ such that $$\langle Tu,v\rangle=\langle u,T^*v\rangle,\quad\forall u,v\in \mathcal H$$In addition we have that $\|T^*\|=\|T\|,(T^*)^*=T$.    
  **Proof**: uniqueness is obvious. For any $v\in\mathcal H$ denote $\ell_v(u)=\langle Tu,v\rangle\in \mathcal H^*$, which is bounded, hence by Riesz representation theorem ([[Topological Dual Space]]) there exists $h=h_v\in\mathcal H$ satisfying $$\braket{Tu,v}=\ell_v(u)=\braket{u,h_v},\quad\forall u\in\mathcal H$$Define $T^*:v\mapsto h_v$, then it's obvious that $T^*\in\mathcal B(\mathcal H)$ satisfying the required property. For norm equivalent just notice that $$\|T\|=\sup_{\|u\|,\|v\|\le1}\left|\braket{Tu,v}\right|=\sup_{\|u\|,\|v\|\le1}\left|\braket{u,T^*v}\right|=\|T^*\|$$Finally $(T^*)^*=T$ is obvious by conjugation property of inner product.
	- **Intuition**: first consider $\rank T=1$, by RRT and definition we have  $$T:u\mapsto\braket{u,\xi}\eta,\quad T^*:v\mapsto\braket{v,\eta}\xi$$that is, the index $\xi$ for $\ell_\xi\in\mathcal H^*$ and the basis $\eta$ of $\im T$ are swapped, and geometrically we're looking some $T^*$ s.t. $\ker T^*=(\im T)^\perp,\im T^*=(\ker T)^\perp$. This generalize directly to $\mathcal R(\mathcal H)$, and since $\mathcal B(\mathcal H)=\overline{\mathcal R(\mathcal H)}$ in **weak convergence** we complete the extension by continuity of inner product. In general, given $T:\mathcal H_1\to\mathcal H_2$, its adjoint $T^*:\mathcal H_1^*\to\mathcal H_2^*$ basically do the following thing: $$T^*\ell=\ell\circ T:u\mapsto \ell(Tu)$$which can be interpreted by the same intuition.
	- $(T+S)^*=T^*+S^*,(TS)^*=S^*T^*$ by definition.
	- **Four fundamental spaces**: since $T^*v=0\iff0=\braket{u,T^*v}=\braket{Tu,v},\forall u$ we have $$(\im T)^\perp=\ker T^*,\quad(\im T^*)^\perp=\ker T$$Further if $\im T$ is closed we can write $\mathcal H=\im T\oplus\ker T^*$.
	- $\|TT^*\|=\|T^*T\|=\|T\|^2=\|T^*\|^2$, which is obvious by definition of norm.
	- **Self-adjoint (symmetric) operator**: $T$ is symmetric if $T=T^*$. In this case we have $\langle Tu,v\rangle\in\mathbb R,\forall u,v\in H$, and $$\|T\|=\sup_{\|u\|=1}|\langle Tu,u\rangle|$$since $\ge$ is obvious while $\le$ can be shown by $$\braket{Tu,v}=\frac{1}{4}(\braket{T(u+v),(u+v)}-\braket{T(u-v),(u-v)})\le\frac{\alpha}{4}(\|u+v\|^2+\|u-v\|^2)=\alpha$$where $\alpha=\sup\left|\braket{Tu,u}\right|$, following the parallelogram law ([[Hilbert Space]]).
		- **Hellinger-Toeplitz Theorem**: symmetric $T$ is bounded.
		  **Proof**: define a family of operators by $$T_u:\mathcal H\to\mathbb C,\quad T_u(v)=\braket{Tu,v},\quad\forall \|u\|=1$$by symmetry ,for fixed $v$ we know that $$\sup_{\|u\|=1}T_u(v)=\braket{u,Tv}\le\|Tv\|<\infty$$hence by UBP ([[Banach Space]]) we have $\sup\|T_u\|<\infty$, which implies $\|T\|<\infty$.
- **Unitary operator**: $U:H\to H'$ is called unitary if it's bijective and preserves norms, i.e., $$\|Uu\|_{H'}=\|u\|_H,\quad\forall u\in H$$or equivalently, $UU^*=U^*U=I$.
- **Isometry**: $T$ is an isometry if $\|Tu\|=\|u\|,\forall u\in\mathcal H$.
	- $\braket{Tu,Tv}=\braket{u,v},\forall u,v\in\mathcal H$ by polarization identity ([[Hilbert Space]]), hence $T^*T=I$. From this we know that $T$ is an isometry iff $T^*T=I$.
	- **Injection**: $T$ is always an injection since $Tu=0\Rightarrow u=0$.
	- **Relation with unitary operator**: the key difference lies in subjectivity. Surjective isometry is unitary, but when itâ€™s not the case (say, the embedding $\mathbb R^n\to\ell^2$) the untouched subspace will be mapped to $0$ by $T^*$. 
- **Finite rank operator**: $T\in\mathcal B(H)$ is of finite rank if $\dim(\im T)<\infty$, denoted $T\in\mathcal R(H)$.
	- **Criterion**: $T\in\mathcal R(H)$ iff there exists $\set{e_n}_{n=1}^L$ orthonormal and $\set{c_{ij}}_{i,j=1}^L\subset\mathbb C$ such that $$Tu=\sum_{i,j=1}^Lc_{ij}\langle u,e_j\rangle e_i,\quad\forall u\in H$$**Proof**: $\Leftarrow$ is obvious. For $\Rightarrow$, consider a basis $\set{\overline e_k}_{k=1}^N$ of $\im T$, then $Tu=\sum_{k=1}^N\langle Tu,\overline e_k\rangle\overline e_k$. If we denote $v_k=T^*\overline e_k$, then the above can be turned into $$Tu=\sum_{k=1}^N\langle u,T^*\overline e_k\rangle\overline e_k=\sum_{k=1}^N\langle u,v_k\rangle\overline e_k$$Now apply Gram-Schmidt process to $\set{\overline e_k,v_k}$ to get $\set{e_n}_{n=1}^L$, then by linearity of inner product we obtain the desired expression.
	- $T\in\mathcal R(H)\iff T^*\in\mathcal R(H)$. Actually by manipulation of terms we have $$Tu=\sum_{i,j=1}^Lc_{ij}\langle u,e_j\rangle e_i\iff T^*v=\sum_{i,j=1}^L\overline{c_{ij}}\langle v,e_i\rangle e_j$$Thus the matrix of $T^*$ can be obtained by taking conjugate transpose of that of $T$.
	- $\mathcal R(H)$ is an **open subspace** of $\mathcal R(H)$, since we can construct a operator series that converges (in norm) to an infinite one by increasing suppression.
- **Compact operator**: $K\in\mathcal B(\mathcal H)$ is compact if $\overline{T(B)}$, where $B$ is the unit ball, is compact. We denote set of compact operators as $\mathcal K(R)$.
	- For $K\in\mathcal K(\mathcal H),S\in\mathcal B(\mathcal H)$ we have $KS,SK\in\mathcal K(\mathcal H)$.
	- $\mathcal K(\mathcal H)=\overline{\mathcal R(\mathcal H)}$: for $\mathcal H$ separable, $T\in\mathcal B(\mathcal H)$ is compact iff there exists $\set{T_n}\subset\mathcal R(\mathcal H)$ such that $\|T-T_n\|\to0$. 
	  **Proof**: given $T=\lim T_n,T_n\in\mathcal K(\mathcal H)$ and any $\set{u_n}\subset\mathcal H$ we can use [[Diagonal Argument]] to construct a converging sub-sequence of $\set{Tu_n}$; conversely given $T\in\mathcal K(\mathcal H)$ we can take an orthonormal basis $\set{e_n}$ and construct $$T_n=P_{\span(e_1,\cdots,e_n)}T \quad\text{or}\quad T_n=TP_{\span(e_1,\cdots,e_n)}$$Both do the job since $\sum\braket{u,e_n}e_n\to u$.
		- For $T$ diagonal w.r.t $\set{\phi_n}$ with $T\phi_n=\lambda_n\phi_n$ it's compact iff $\lambda_n\to0$.
	- **Characterization with weak convergence**: $K$ is compact iff $K(\set{u_n})$ with weakly convergent $\set{u_n}$ is strongly convergent.
	- $T\in\mathcal K\iff T^*\in\mathcal K\iff T^*T\in\mathcal K$: we only need to approximate $T$ by $T_n\in\mathcal R(\mathcal H)$ and consider $T_n^*$ or $T^*T_n$. The converse is similar.
- **Hilbert-Schmidt operator**: H-S operator is any $T\in\mathcal B(\mathcal H)$ that has finite H-S norm $$\|T\|_{HS}^2=\sum\|Te_n\|^2$$where $\set{e_n}$ is an orthonormal basis.
	- **Well-defined-ness of norm**: given two bases $\set{e_i},\set{f_j}$ we have $$\sum_i\|Te_i\|^2=\sum_{i,j}\left|\braket{Te_i,f_j}\right|^2=\sum_{i,j}\left|\braket{e_i,T^*f_j}\right|^2=\sum_j\|T^*f_j\|^2$$taking $e_i=f_i$ yields $\sum\|Te_i\|^2=\sum\|T^*e_i\|^2$, hence the last terms equals $\sum_j\|Tf_j\|^2$.
	- **Compactness**: notice that $T_n=TP_{\span(e_1,\cdots,e_n)}$ are compact, and $\|T_n-T\|\to0$ by Cauchy-Schwarz inequality.
