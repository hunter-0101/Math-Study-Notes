Below we adopt the definition and notations from [[Series]].
**Fundamental theorem of asymptotic analysis**: if $x_n\sim y_n$ and either sequence of partial sum tends to $\infty$, then both partial sum sequences tend to $\infty$ and are asymptotic.
- **Discrete Picard-Lindelöf**: assuming that the recurrence relation is a contraction mapping we can apply the spirit of Picard-Lindelöf theorem ([[First Order ODE]]) to discrete case.
	- **Example**: consider the recurrence relation $$a_{n+1}=a_n-\frac{1}{n+1}\sin a_n,\quad a_0\in[0,\pi/2]$$It's easy to show that $a_n\searrow0,(n+1)a_{n+1}>na_n$. Since $\sin x\ge\varepsilon x,\forall x\in[0,a_0]$ for some $0<\varepsilon<1$, we can show by induction that $$a_n=O\left(\frac{1}{n^{\varepsilon}}\right)$$ Meanwhile, by Taylor expansion we have $$a_{n+1}\le a_n-\frac{1}{n+1}\left(a_n-\frac{1}{6}a_n^3\right)\quad\Longrightarrow\quad(n+1)a_{n+1}-na_{n}=\frac{1}{6}a_n^3$$by repetitive substitution and summation we could improve the asymptotic estimation to $$a_n=O\left(\frac{1}{n^{3^k\varepsilon}}\right),\quad\forall 3^k\varepsilon<1$$Similarly we can improve the result further via proper approximation.
- **Asymptotic integral**: let $f(x)$ be a continuous and monotonic for large $x$. Provided $f(x)\sim f(x+1)$ and $\int_1^n f(x)dx\to\infty$, then $$\sum_{k=1}^nf(k)\sim\int_1^nf(x)dx$$
	- **Application**: show that $\sum\frac{a^k}{k}\sim\frac{a}{a-1}\frac{a^n}{n}$.
	  **Proof**: we only have to show that $$\sum_{k=1}^n\frac{a^k}{k}\sim\frac{\ln a}{a-1}\int_1^{n+1}\frac{a^u}{u}du\quad\text{and}\quad\int_1^{n+1}\frac{a^u}{u}du\sim\frac{a}{\ln a}\frac{a^n}{n}$$Both of the estimations above can be shown with L'Hôpital's theorem.
- **Summation by parts**: given $(a_n)$ and $a<b$, denote $S(t)=\sum_{a<n\le t}$, then for $f\in C([a,b])$, $$\sum_{a<n\le b}a_nf(n)=S(b)f(b)-\int_a^bS(t)f'(t)dt$$the proof is straightforward by the ordinary summation by parts formula below.
	- **Euler's summation formula**: take $a_n=1$, then $S(t)=[t]-[a]$, and we have $$\sum_{a<n\le b}f(n)=\int_a^bf(t)dt+\int_a^bf'(t)\psi(t)dt+f(a)\psi(a)-f(b)\psi(b)$$where $\psi(x)=x-[x]-\frac{1}{2}$.
## Other methods
- **Stirling's formula**: As $n\to\infty$, we have $$n!\sim\left(\frac{n}{e}\right)^n\sqrt{2\pi n}$$**Proof**: we know that $n!=\int_0^\infty x^ne^{-x}dx$ (from [[Gamma Function]]), consider the analogy between the graphs of $y=x^ne^{-x}$ and standard [[Normal Distribution]], we make the change of variable $t=(x-n)/\sqrt{n}$. We get $$n!=\int_{-\sqrt{n}}^\infty(n+\sqrt{n}t)^ne^{-(n+\sqrt{n}t)}\sqrt{n}dt=\frac{n^n\sqrt{n}}{e^n}\int_{-\sqrt{n}}^\infty\left(1+\frac{t}{\sqrt{n}}\right)^ne^{-\sqrt{n}t}dt$$Thus it suffice to show that the remaining integral tends to $\sqrt{2\pi}$. Based on Gaussian integral, we only need to show $$\left(1+\frac{t}{\sqrt{n}}\right)^ne^{-\sqrt{n}t}\to e^{-t^2/2},\quad\forall t\in R$$which is obviously correct by the definition of $e$.
	- A weaker version is $\ln n!\sim n\ln n$, which can be proven by $n\ln n-n<\ln n!<n\ln n$. A more accurate version is $$n!=\left(\frac{n}{e}\right)^n\sqrt{2\pi n}\left[1+O\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)\right]$$
- **Laplace's method**: The motivation is that for $f$ twice continuously differentiable on $[a,b]$ and global maximum $f(x_0)=\max_{x\in[a,b]} f(x)$ the ratio $e^{Mf(x_0)}/e^{Mf(x)}=e^{M(f(x_0)-f(x))}$ decays exponentially as $M\to\infty$, while $f(x_0)/f(x)$ stays unchanged, thus it makes sense to approximate $e^{Mf(x)}$ with $f(x)=f(x_0)-\frac{1}{2}|f''(x_0)|(x-x_0)^2$. Formally we have $$\int_a^be^{Mf(x)}dx\sim e^{Mf(x_0)}\sqrt\frac{2\pi}{M(-f''(x_0))},\quad M\to\infty$$this holds if $a=-\infty$ or $b=\infty$ (or both), and can be proven in the first principle.
	- Sometimes we also write $$\int_a^b h(x)e^{Mf(x)}dx\sim\sqrt\frac{2\pi}{M|f''(x_0)|}h(x_0)e^{Mf(x_0)},\quad M\to\infty$$In this case the accuracy depends on the variable of integration.
