- **Why frequency splitting works**: many of the problems we care about are invariant under some special operators like translation, scaling, dilation, etc., and FT turns out to be tailored for them ([[FT on L2]]).
## Methods for realization
Below we use a simple example to illustrate two ways to realize frequency splitting.
**Gagliardoâ€“Nirenberg type inequality**: assuming validity of all terms below, show that $$\|f'\|_1^2\le r\|f\|_1\|f''\|_1,\quad r\in\mathbb R$$For simplicity we denote the above inequality as $B^2\le rAC$.
**Proof 1 (mollifier smoothing)**: pick a proper [[Mollifier]] $\rho$, and denote $\rho_h(x)=h^{-1}\rho(h^{-1}x)$. Write $$f'=\rho_h*f'+(f'-\rho_h*f')\quad\Longrightarrow\quad B\le\|\rho_h*f'\|_1+\|f'-\rho_h*f'\|_1$$For the smoothed part, by [[Convolution]] we have $$\|\rho_h*f'\|_1=\|\rho_h'*f\|_1\le\|\rho_h'\|_1\cdot\|f\|_1=\frac{\|\rho'\|_1}{h}A$$For the higher-frequency part, utilizing Fubini ([[Lebesgue Integral]]) and some standard results, $$\begin{align}\|f'-\rho_h*f'\|_1&=\int\rho_h(y)(f'(x)-f'(x-y))dy\\&\le\int\rho_h(y)\int_\mathbb R|f'(x)-f'(x-y)|dxdy&\text{by triangle inequality}\\&=\int\rho_h(y)\int_\mathbb R\left|\int_{x-y}^x f''(t)dt\right|dxdy&\text{by FTC}\\&\le\int\rho_h(y)\int_\mathbb R\int_{x-y}^x|f''(t)|dtdxdy&\text{by triangle inequality}\\&=\int\rho_h(y)|y|\int_\mathbb R|f''(t)|dt&\text{by Fubini's theorem}\\&=C\int\rho_h(y)|y|dy\end{align}$$Via simple calculation we have that $$\int\rho_h(y)|y|dy=h\int|z|\rho(z)dz\coloneqq hm_1$$then finally we get the estimation $$B\le\frac{\|\rho'\|_1}{h}A+hm_1C$$now by amplification technique we can take an optimal $h$ to get $$B^2\le 4\|\rho'\|_1m_1AC$$Now we can properly pick $\rho$ to yield an explicit coefficient of the estimation.
**Proof 2 (Fourier truncation)**: by [[FT on L1]] we have $$\widehat{f'}(\xi)=i\xi\hat f(\xi),\quad\widehat{f''}(\xi)=-(\xi^2)\hat f(\xi)$$fix a frequency cutoff $R>0$, and write $$\hat f_\text{low}=\chi_{|\xi|\le R}(\xi)\hat f(\xi),\quad\hat f_\text{high}(\xi)=\chi_{|\xi|>R}\hat f(\xi)$$and accordingly $f'=f'_\text{low}+f'_\text{high}$ (here $\chi_{|\xi|\le R}+\chi_{|\xi|>R}=1$ do no always indicate sharp truncation, and **must be chosen carefully**, otherwise $K_R$ defined below may fail to be integrable. We still adopt the name "truncation" for consistency). Now for the low frequency component, we have $$f'_\text{low}(x)=\int_{|\xi|\le R}i\xi\hat f(\xi)e^{i\xi x}d\xi=(K_R*f)(x)\quad\text{where}\quad K_R(x)=\mathcal F^{-1}[i\xi\chi_{|\xi|\le R}](x)$$then notice two important facts ([[FT on L2]]) $$K_R(x)=RK_1(Rx)\quad\Longrightarrow\quad \|K_R\|_1=\|K_1\|_1$$therefore by Young's convolution inequality ([[Lp Space]]) we have $$\|f'_\text{low}\|_1=\|K_R*f\|_1\le\|K_R\|_1\|f\|_1\le C_1R\|f\|_1$$For high frequency component, we have $$f'_\text{high}(x)=\mathcal F^{-1}[i\xi\chi_{|\xi|>R}\hat f(\xi)](x)=\mathcal F^{-1}\left[(-i/\xi)\chi_{|\xi|>R}\widehat{f''}(\xi)\right](x)=(L_R*f'')(x)$$again $L_R$ share the same properties as $K_R$, and we have $$\|f'_\text{high}\|_1=\|L_R*f''\|_1\le\|L_R\|_1\|f''\|_1\le\frac{C_2}{R}\|f''\|_1$$the rest are the same - we optimize $R$ to get an optimal bound.
Some related discussions are provided below.
- **Why frequency splitting works**: in the above example, from the perspective of $f'$ we have $$\begin{array}{l}f&\text{integration}&\text{measure the amplitude}\\f''&\text{differentiation}&\text{measure the oscillation}\end{array}$$More concretely, as discussed in [[FT on L2]] we have $$\widehat{D^sf}(\xi)=(i\xi)^s\hat f(\xi)$$When estimating $\|D^\sigma f\|_p$ in terms of $\|f\|_p$ and $\|D^\tau f\|_p$ with $0\le\sigma\le\tau$, splitting at $|\xi|=R$: $$\begin{cases}|\xi|^\sigma\le R^\sigma,&\text{if }|\xi|\le R\\ |\xi|^\sigma\le R^{\sigma-\tau}|\xi|^\tau,&\text{if }|\xi|>R\end{cases}$$Passing back to physical space and estimating each piece yields $$\|D^\sigma f\|_p\lesssim R^\sigma\|f\|_p+R^{\sigma-\tau}\|D^\tau f\|_p$$then optimizing $R$ gives the usual interpolation inequality.
- **Comparison between two methods**: intuitively, mollifier smoothing is equivalent to truncation in frequency domain. More rigorously, if we check the correspondence $$\begin{array}&&&\text{low frequency}&&\text{high frequency}\\\hline\text{mollifier smoothing}&f'=&\rho_h*f'&+&f-\rho_h*f'\\\text{Fourier truncation}&f'=&\mathcal F^{-1}\left(\chi_{|\xi|\le R}\widehat{f'}\right)&+&\mathcal F^{-1}\left(\chi_{|\xi|>R}\widehat{f'}\right)\end{array}$$then by the [[Convolution]] theorem we have $$\mathcal F\left(\rho_h*f'\right)=\mathcal F(\rho_h)\cdot\mathcal F(f')\quad\xleftrightarrow{\quad\large?\quad}\quad\chi_{|\xi|\le R}\cdot\mathcal F(f')$$so both are actually applying a Fourier multiplier, but that mollifier uses a smooth multiplier $m_h(\xi)=\hat\rho(h\xi)$ which is $\approx1$ for $|\xi|\ll1/h$ and decays for $|\xi|\gg1/h$, while the sharp truncation is just a clean, discontinuous $\chi_{|\xi|\le R}$.
	- **When the second method fails**: sometimes the $K_R$ defined above is not integrable, hence the bound above fails. In such case one possible fix is to pick a more smooth truncation function rather than the sharp $\chi_{|\xi|\le R}$. An explicit example is provided in [[Green's Function]].
- **Algebraic perspective**: for multiplication with a cutoff $\phi$, denoted $M_\phi$, we have $$[D,M_\phi]=D\circ M_\phi-M_\phi\circ D=M_{\phi'}$$by the chain rule.
## Other examples
- **Nash inequality**: show that there exists a universal constant $K$ such that, for all $C^1$ function $f:\mathbb R^2\to\mathbb R$, if $f\in L^1\cap L^2,|\nabla f|\in L^2$, then $$\|f\|_2^2\le K\|f\|_1\|\nabla f\|_2$$**Proof**: we have that $\|f\|_2=\|\hat f\|_2,\|\nabla f\|_2=\||\xi|\cdot\hat f\|_2$. Now given any $R>0$, we have $$\begin{align}\|\hat f\|_2^2&=\left(\int_{|\xi|<R}+\int_{|\xi|\ge R}\right)|\hat f(\xi)|^2d\xi\\&\le \|f\|_1^2\cdot\pi R^2+\frac{1}{R^2}|\xi\hat f(\xi)|^2\\&=\pi R^2\|f\|_1^2+\frac{1}{R^2}\|\nabla f\|_2^2\end{align}$$By AG inequality the condition for optimal $R$ is $$\pi R^2\|f\|_1^2=\frac{1}{R^2}\|\nabla f\|_2^2\quad\iff\quad R^2=\frac{\|\nabla f\|_2}{\sqrt\pi\|f\|_1}$$Putting this back into the inequality yields the coefficient $K=2\sqrt\pi$.
	- **Generalized Nash inequality**: There exists some constant $C>0$ s.t. $$\|u\|_2^{1+2/n}\le C\|u\|_1^{2/n}\|\Delta u\|_2,\quad\forall u\in L^1(\mathbb R^n)\cap W^{1,2}(\mathbb R^n)$$The proof is entirely similar.
