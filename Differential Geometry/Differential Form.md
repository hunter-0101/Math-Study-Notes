Given a [[Smooth Manifold]] $M$, the **cotangent space** at $p\in M$ is defined as the [[Topological Dual Space]] of the [[Tangent Bundle]] $$T_p^*M=\paren{T_pM}^*$$whose elements are **covectors** ([[Exterior Algebra]]). The **cotangent bundle** is the union $$T^*M=\bigcup_{p\in M}T_p^*M$$which is a [[Vector Bundle]] over $M$. Any section ([[Fiber Bundle]]) $\omega\in T^*M$ is called a **differential 1-form**, or also called a **covector field**. It's said to be smooth ($C^\infty$) if it's a smooth section. 
- **[[Module]] structure**: $T^*M$ forms a module over the ring of [[Smooth Map]] $C^\infty(M)$ with $$\omega(fX)=f\omega(X),\quad\forall f\in C^\infty(M),X\in TM$$
- **Differential of a function**: given $f\in C^\infty(M,\mathbb R)$ define its differential as the 1-form $$(df)_p(X_p)=X_pf$$then viewing $\mathbb R$ as a smooth manifold, by definition above we have $$f_*(X_p)=(df)_p(X_p)\left.\frac{d}{dr}\right|_{f(p)},\quad\forall X_p\in T_pM$$hence the newly defined differential can be identified with that for general maps via $$a\left.\frac{d}{dr}\right|_{f(p)}\longleftrightarrow a$$For this reason we call both of them "differential". 
- **Pullback of 1-form**: for $F\in C^\infty(M,N)$ define its **co-differential** as $$F^*=(F_*)^*:T^*N\to T^*M,\quad F^*(\omega_{F(p)})(X_p)=((F_*)^*\omega_{F(p)})(X_p)=\omega_{F(p)}(F_* X_p)$$and call $F^*(\omega_{F(p)})$ the **pullback** of $\omega_{F(p)}\in T^*N$. Note that every 1-form can be pulled back, where vector field does not. This is mainly due to the asymmetry of function, illustrated below

	- **Pullback of function**: for $g\in C^\infty(N)$ we define $F^*g=g\circ F\in C^\infty(M)$.
	- **Commutation with other operations**: we can easily show via definition that $$F^*(dh)=d(F^*h),\quad F^*(\omega+\tau)=F^*(\omega)+F^*(\tau),\quad F^*(h\omega)=(F^*h)(F^*\omega)$$for any $h\in C^\infty(N),\omega,\tau\in T^*N$. From these properties we further notice that $$F^*\omega=F^*\left(\sum_j a_jdx^j\right)=\sum_j(F^*a_j)(F^*dx^j)=\sum_j(a_j\circ F)d(x^j\circ F)=\sum_{i,j}(a_j\circ F)\partial_i F^jdx^i$$hence pullback of smooth 1-form is also a smooth 1-form.
	- **Restriction of 1-form**: for $S\subset M$ an immersed submanifold define $\omega|_S$ the restricted form. Denote $\iota$ the inclusion map, then it's obvious that $\omega|_S=\iota^*\omega$.
## Differential $k$-form
Consider the alternating operator $A_k$ defined in [[Exterior Algebra]], set $$A_k(T_pM)=\bigwedge^{k}(T_p^*M)$$and call $\omega_p\in A_k(T_pM)$ a **$k$-covector**. A **differential $k$-form** is a $k$-covector field. Smooth $k$-form is a smooth section of the [[Vector Bundle]] $\bigwedge^k(T^*M)$, denoted as $$\Omega^k(M)=\Gamma\left(\bigwedge^\nolimits{k}(T^*M)\right)$$Those with $\deg\omega=\dim M$ is called a **top form**.
- **Multi-linearity**: for $\omega\in \bigwedge^k(T^*M)$ we have that $$\omega(X_1,\cdots,hX_i,\cdots,X_k)=h\omega(X_1,\cdots,X_k),\quad\forall h\in C^\infty(M)$$as a corollary of linearity of 1-form over $C^\infty(M)$.
- **Basis**: by [[Exterior Algebra]] the set $dx^I,I=(i_1<\cdots< i^k)$ forms a basis for $\bigwedge^k(T^*M)$. Moreover, since $\partial_Ix^I=\delta_i^J$, denoting $\mathfrak I=\set{I=(i_1<\cdots< i^k)}$ then $$df^I=df^1\wedge\cdots\wedge df^k=\sum_{I\in\mathfrak I}\partial_If^I dx^I,\quad\partial_If^I=\det J_{f^I}$$which can be easily shown by linearity.
- **Pullback of $k$-form**: for $F\in C^\infty(M,N)$ define the pullback as $$F^*:A_k(T_{F(p)}N)\to A_k(T_pM),\quad F^*(\omega_{F(p)})(v_1,\cdots,v_k)=\omega_{F(p)}(F_*v_1,\cdots,F_*v_k),\quad v_i\in T_pM$$It's obvious that $F^*$ is linear over $\mathbb R$. Using the commutation property below, for a $C^\infty$ k-form $\omega=\sum a_Idy^I$ we have $$\begin{align}F^\omega&=\sum_I(F^*a_I)(F^*dy^{i_1})\wedge\cdots\wedge (F^*dy^{i_k})\\&=\sum_I(a_I\circ F)dF^{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge dF^{i_k}\\&=\sum_{I,J}(a_I\circ F)\frac{\partial(F^{i_1},\cdots,F^{i_k})}{\partial(x^{j_1},\cdots,x^{j_k})}dx^J\end{align}$$hence we have $F^*\omega\in C^\infty$.
- **Wedge product**: we define pointwise $(\omega\wedge\tau)_p=\omega_p\wedge\tau_p$, then by direct calculation we can show that $F^*(\omega\wedge\tau)=F^*\omega\wedge F^*\tau$.
## Exterior derivative
An **exterior derivative** on $M$ is a $\mathbb R$-linear map $$D:\Omega^\bullet (M)\to\Omega^\bullet(M)$$s.t. $D$ is an anti-derivation of degree $1$ ([[Modern Calculus on Euclidean Space]]), $D\circ D=0$, and $(Df)(X)=Xf$ for any $f\in C^\infty,X\in\mathfrak X$. Note that the third condition ensures $D=d$ the differential of a function when restricted to $\Omega^0(M)$.
- **Locality**: $D$ is a local operator in the sense that, whenever $\omega\in \Omega^*(M)$ restricts to $0$ on open $U$ then $D\omega=0$ on $U$. This can be shown by calculating $D(f\omega)$ where $f$ is a bump function.
- **Existence & uniqueness**: given local chart $(U,x^i)$ it's easy to deduce that $$d_U\omega=\sum da_I\wedge dx^I$$which is independent of choice of chart by uniqueness of local expression of form. By induction we can show $D=d$ as in [[Differential Calculus on Smooth Manifold]].
	- **Commutation with pullback**: for $F\in C^\infty(M,N),\omega\in\Omega^*(M)$ we have that $dF^*\omega=F^*d\omega$. This can shown using commutation of $F^*$ with differential of functions and wedge product.
